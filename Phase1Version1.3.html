Phase 1 on a Page. Laying the Groundwork
Framework. Current Conditions and Readiness Framework. Evidence and Learning Loop Framework. Phase 1 Key Outcome
Goal. Build an honest picture of how learning and assessment feel today, so the local accountability model is grounded in reality, not guesses.
In plain language
  • We tell the truth about what school feels like right now for students, families, and staff.
  • We gather real examples of learning and assessment, not only test scores and rankings.
  • We create a Local Accountability Advisory Council so every stakeholder group has a seat at the table.
  • We decide, together, where we are ready to grow and where we need to build foundations first.
By the end of Phase 1, we have:
  • A one page summary for each school that captures strengths, challenges, and readiness.
  • A district wide picture of where we are starting from that the board and community can see.
  • Three to five clear priorities that will drive Phase 2 design and decisions.
  • A functioning Local Accountability Advisory Council that understands its role and next steps.
Who needs to understand this
  • Board members and district leaders who set direction and expectations.
  • Principals and teacher leaders who organize the work and gather evidence.
  • Staff, families, community partners, and students who will live the changes.
Most people only need this view. The detailed steps, checklists, and LAAC tools are for the teams actually running Phase 1.
From a student perspective
  • I know adults are asking honest questions about what school feels like for students, not only about test scores.
  • I have more than one way to share my voice, such as a survey, panel, classroom reflection, student council, or advisory conversation.
  • I see evidence that adults are acting on student voice, like a summary shared back to students and a clear next step.
  • I understand that Phase 1 is about the system improving, not students being judged.
Student example
Eighth grade students are invited to a lunch conversation where the principal asks, "When do you feel like your learning really matters" and "What makes it hard to show what you really know". Notes from the conversation are shared with the Local Accountability Advisory Council, then posted back to students with three themes and two actions.
From a teacher perspective
  • I understand why we are doing Phase 1 and how it connects to what students experience in my classroom.
  • I have provided sample units, tasks, and student work that show both strong learning and common constraints.
  • I have participated in a short sensemaking conversation about grading, assessment, relevance, and student voice.
  • I know what will happen with the evidence we submit, who reviews it, and how it will shape Phase 2 decisions.
Teacher example
In a PLC, teachers bring recent assessment tasks and student work. The group identifies which tasks allow students to demonstrate thinking and which ones mainly reward compliance. The PLC writes two recommendations for Phase 2, including one change they can pilot now.
From a staff member perspective
  • I understand Phase 1 is about improving the student experience, not blaming staff members.
  • I have a simple way to share what I see in my role, including patterns I notice over time.
  • I know what to say when a parent asks, "What is local accountability" and where to point them for a simple explanation.
  • I can name at least one thing leaders are paying attention to because of input from support staff.
Staff example
Transportation and cafeteria teams share short weekly notes on student energy, behavior hotspots, and communication breakdowns they observe. Those notes become part of the school summary under conditions that support or hinder learning.
From a school leader perspective
  • I can explain Phase 1 in two or three sentences to any staff member, student, or parent.
  • I have an actual calendar for evidence collection, listening sessions, ratings, and summary completion, with owners for each task.
  • I know how Phase 1 connects to the Six Pillars, the Continuum, and DDIER, and I can show that connection in one simple visual.
  • I am protecting staff time by using existing structures, like PLCs and faculty meetings, instead of adding separate meetings.
School leader example
During a faculty meeting, the principal shares a one page Phase 1 calendar and a short evidence list. Teachers leave knowing exactly what is needed, when it is due, and how it will be discussed and used.
From a district leader perspective
  • We have communicated expectations and supports clearly, including what is required and what is flexible.
  • We have a plan to synthesize school findings into a district story that is honest, understandable, and useful.
  • We can name exactly how Phase 1 findings will influence Phase 2 decisions, including what will stop, start, or be redesigned.
  • We are actively scanning for equity and consistency so Phase 1 is not stronger in some buildings only because of capacity.
District example
The district team provides a "Phase 1 evidence menu" with examples and a shared naming system. They assign a point person to each school, run short weekly office hours, and track participation so no school falls behind quietly.
From a board member perspective
  • I have seen a short, plain-language overview of Phase 1 goals and how it fits into the long range accountability work.
  • I understand Phase 1 is about learning how the system is working, not evaluating individual teachers.
  • I know when I will see a Phase 1 synthesis, what questions to ask, and how LAAC recommendations will be presented.
  • I am prepared to support the next step by aligning policy, resources, and public messaging with what Phase 1 shows.
Board example
At a work session, leaders share three things Phase 1 will reveal, three things it will not do, and the exact board decision points that will come later. Board members ask clarifying questions that keep the focus on learner experience and evidence.
From a parent or guardian perspective
  • I have heard, in simple language, what the district is doing in Phase 1 and why it matters for my child.
  • I have more than one easy option to share my experience, such as a survey, a listening session, or a quick phone check-in.
  • I believe my feedback will shape what happens next because I see a summary shared back and at least one action taken.
  • I understand how this work can improve grading clarity, communication, and real-world learning opportunities.
Parent example
The school sends a short family note with three questions about communication, grading clarity, and student engagement. Results are shared back with "what we heard" and "what we will do next" within two weeks.
From a community member perspective
  • I understand the district is building a local accountability model that goes beyond test scores.
  • I know how local businesses, organizations, and partners can share input about readiness and real-world skills.
  • I have seen communication that connects this work to community priorities, workforce needs, and the future of the community.
  • I can name one way I can support learning, such as mentorships, authentic audiences, internships, or project partnerships.
Community example
A local employer sits on the LAAC and offers two specific insights. One about communication habits and one about problem-solving. Those insights become criteria the district later uses when designing performance evidence.
The Three Big Moves in Phase 1
Instead of many separate tasks, think of Phase 1 as three big moves that everyone can name and remember.
Big Move 1
Launch the Story and Form the Council
  • Explain why the district is moving toward local accountability.
  • Connect Phase 1 to learner experience, not compliance or rankings.
  • Form the LAAC with representation from each stakeholder group.
  • Set expectations for how LAAC feedback will be used and shared back.
Output. A shared message and a functioning LAAC that will walk with the district through all phases.
Big Move 2
Look at Reality Together
  • Gather real examples of teaching, learning, and assessment across schools.
  • Listen to students, staff, and families about their lived experience.
  • Use a simple readiness rubric to rate a few key domains consistently.
  • Surface equity gaps in opportunities, expectations, and evidence.
Output. A clear, honest picture of strengths and gaps in each school that the LAAC can review and respond to.
Big Move 3
Choose Priorities and Next Steps
  • Synthesize Phase 1 findings into a one page school profile.
  • Identify three to five focus areas for Phase 2 design work.
  • Create a district wide starting picture and review it with the LAAC.
  • Close Phase 1 with clear next steps and clear ownership.
Output. School and district summaries that point Phase 2 in the right direction, validated by the LAAC.
Stakeholder Stage Set. What Phase 1 Often Feels Like
These boxes set the stage for Phase 1. They describe what people may be experiencing and what Phase 1 asks from them. Use them to communicate with clarity and reduce confusion before the work begins.
Phase 1, through each stakeholder
Quick reference
Students
Common reality and what Phase 1 changes
  • You might feel known for grades and tests more than your strengths and growth.
  • You might have strong projects, but they do not count as real evidence.
  • Phase 1 asks you to name what helps you learn and what blocks you.
  • You should see your feedback shared back, plus at least one action.
Teachers
Common reality and what Phase 1 changes
  • You might feel initiative fatigue or mixed messages about what matters.
  • You might have high-quality learning, but inconsistent grading and assessment norms.
  • Phase 1 asks you to bring real artifacts and describe constraints honestly.
  • You should see priorities narrowed, not a bigger to-do list.
Staff Members
Common reality and what Phase 1 changes
  • You see student stress, routines, and communication breakdowns up close.
  • You are often asked to support change without being asked for your insight.
  • Phase 1 asks what you notice, where systems fail, and what support is missing.
  • You should get simple talking points for families and a clear contact.
School Leaders
Common reality and what Phase 1 changes
  • You may be balancing improvement with daily operational pressure.
  • You may have uneven implementation across teams and grade levels.
  • Phase 1 asks you to organize evidence, listening, and readiness ratings.
  • You should exit Phase 1 with a clear school profile and priorities.
District Leaders
Common reality and what Phase 1 changes
  • You may have lots of data but limited evidence of authentic learning.
  • You may have inconsistent practice across schools and programs.
  • Phase 1 asks you to create coherence, tools, coaching, and synthesis.
  • You should exit with a district story, validated by the LAAC, and design targets.
Board Members
Common reality and what Phase 1 changes
  • You may feel pressure to respond to scores, headlines, or comparisons.
  • You may want clearer indicators that match community values.
  • Phase 1 asks you to learn the system story before making big moves.
  • You should see a Phase 1 synthesis plus LAAC recommendations in plain language.
Parents and Guardians
Common reality and what Phase 1 changes
  • You may get grades, but not clarity on what your child can actually do.
  • You may want more relevant learning and stronger communication.
  • Phase 1 asks you to describe what you see, especially about support and clarity.
  • You should see feedback shared back, plus one concrete improvement action.
Community Members
Common reality and what Phase 1 changes
  • You may value test scores, but also readiness, work ethic, and contribution.
  • You may want stronger ties between school learning and community life.
  • Phase 1 asks you to define readiness indicators and authentic opportunities.
  • You should see a clear invite to support learning through real audiences and partnerships.
How to use these boxes
If a stakeholder group is confused or skeptical, start here. Name the reality. Explain what Phase 1 will do. Then show what they will see back.

1. Orientation to Phase 1
Why Phase 1 exists, what success looks like, and how Smith County leaders can use this guide as a working roadmap instead of a static document.
Purpose of Phase 1
Start with reality
The purpose of Phase 1 is to help Smith County build an honest, evidence based picture of current conditions and readiness across all schools, with a Local Accountability Advisory Council that reflects every stakeholder group.
  • Describe the current learner experience with real artifacts and data.
  • Identify strengths that can be leveraged in the local accountability design.
  • Surface gaps, inequities, and pain points that must be addressed.
  • Determine readiness levels at both school and district levels.
  • Agree on a small number of priorities that will guide Phase 2 design work.
  • Establish and activate the LAAC as a standing partner throughout all phases.
Who This Guide Is For
Primary users
This guide is written for people who will actually do the work of Phase 1 in Smith County Schools, KY.
  • Superintendent and central office leaders who frame the work and protect time for it.
  • District accountability, curriculum, and assessment leads who design tools and coach schools.
  • Principals and assistant principals who organize and lead Phase 1 in each building.
  • Teacher leaders and PLC facilitators who bring classroom evidence and insight.
  • Program directors for special education, multilingual learners, counseling, and other services.
  • Members of the Local Accountability Advisory Council who represent students, families, staff, and community.
How to use this guide
Use this guide as a living playbook. Print key sections for leadership meetings, link it in your local accountability site, and mark it up as you learn from practice. The view toggle at the top lets you move between an Everyone view and a Leader Edition.
Entry Point. How Schools and Districts Usually Begin
Phase 1 starts at a real starting line. Choose the entry point that best matches your current reality, then use Phase 1 to convert that effort into evidence, shared understanding, and clear priorities.
Select your most likely starting line
Major Step in Phase 1
What this entry point usually looks like
  • You already have a Portrait of a Learner/Graduate, Deeper Learning competencies, or a durable skills framework.
  • Staff can describe the vision, but the evidence system does not consistently capture it.
  • Students experience strong moments of authentic learning, but it is uneven across classrooms and courses.
Phase 1 purpose from this starting line
Turn a vision into a clear baseline. Identify where the portrait is already visible in real work, where it is not, and what prevents it.
Starting line signals
You see agency in pockets, but reporting still leans on grades and scores as the main record.
Phase 1 scans for artifacts of the portrait and how often they are captured.
What to collect first
6 to 10 strong artifacts that clearly show portrait outcomes. Student work, performance tasks, reflection prompts.
Keep it tight. Quality and clarity beat volume.
Common pitfall
Celebrating portrait language while students cannot prove it with evidence that counts.
Phase 1 asks: What is visible, what is invisible, and why.
LAAC role
Confirm whether the portrait describes real experience. Name what matters most locally and how it should be shown publicly.
The LAAC keeps the portrait grounded and locally owned.
Ready for Phase 2 if
You can point to real student evidence of portrait outcomes, name gaps clearly, and define 3 to 5 priorities to build an evidence and reporting system that matches the portrait.
What this entry point usually looks like
  • You already have improvement plans, goals, and action steps.
  • Data cycles exist, but can feel compliance-driven and disconnected from authentic learning evidence.
  • Staff are used to reviewing numbers, but less used to reviewing student work as evidence.
Phase 1 purpose from this starting line
Convert improvement work into a stronger evidence loop. Add learner experience and multiple measures so improvement is about learning, not only outputs.
Starting line signals
You have goals and rhythms, but struggle to show what students can do beyond the scoreboard.
Phase 1 broadens evidence so improvement gets more actionable.
What to collect first
Evidence anchors for each goal. One student work artifact, one practice indicator, one perception indicator.
This makes cycles less abstract and more real.
Common pitfall
Running the same cycle with the same narrow data, then expecting different results.

LAAC role
Interpret whether priorities match lived experience and help choose measures the public understands and trusts.
The LAAC strengthens legitimacy and clarity.
Ready for Phase 2 if
Improvement work includes multiple measures, student work is part of review, and the district can explain reporting in plain language.
What this entry point usually looks like
  • You are starting or refreshing a strategic plan.
  • Engagement is active, but the plan risks being broad without a learner evidence baseline.
  • Staff want alignment and focus, and the district wants clearer reporting of progress.
Phase 1 purpose from this starting line
Build a baseline that prevents aspirational planning from drifting away from real evidence.
Starting line signals
You have committees and goal drafts, but cannot show where you are right now with clear evidence.
Phase 1 becomes your baseline before commitments lock in.
What to collect first
Current conditions scan plus two listening cycles (students and families) to reflect lived experience.
This reduces the gap between vision and reality.
Common pitfall
Writing a strategic plan and calling it accountability.
Phase 1 asks: What counts as evidence, and who will trust it.
LAAC role
A standing community table that sustains feedback beyond a one-time engagement event.
The LAAC prevents engage and disappear.
Ready for Phase 2 if
The plan has measures tied to learner experience, the baseline is credible, and reporting is understandable and locally validated.
A practical test
If you cannot answer "What is our starting line, and what evidence proves it" in one minute, you are still at the entry point. Phase 1 is the bridge to clarity.
2. Preconditions and Readiness to Launch Phase 1
A quick check for Smith County to confirm that minimum conditions are in place before asking schools and the LAAC to begin.
Minimum District Readiness
Before launch
District leaders should review these items before launching Phase 1.
  • We can explain in two or three sentences why the district is moving toward a local accountability model and how it connects to learner experience.
  • We identified our most likely entry point and explained why it fits our current reality.
  • We introduced the Six Pillars, the Local Accountability Continuum, and the DDIER cycle to school leaders at a basic level.
  • We identified a Phase 1 district lead team and clarified roles for accountability, curriculum, assessment, and school support.
  • We designed draft templates for the Current Conditions Scan, the Readiness Rubric, and the School Phase 1 Summary Profile.
  • We drafted a plan for how the LAAC will be formed, how members will be selected, and how often it will meet during Phase 1.
  • We have a clear plan for how schools will submit artifacts and how the district will organize and access them.
LAAC focus in this stage
The LAAC chair or co-chairs are identified, and a short charter is drafted that defines the council purpose in Phase 1, even before the full membership is finalized.

3. Roles, Responsibilities, and the Local Accountability Advisory Council
Clear lanes for who does what in Phase 1 so work is collaborative but not confusing, with the LAAC as a visible connector.
System Level Roles
District and board
Superintendent and district leaders
  • Frame the purpose of Phase 1 publicly and consistently, including why the LAAC matters.
  • Protect time for schools to do the work without feeling rushed or overloaded.
  • Provide tools, coaching, and feedback to principals and school teams.
  • Coordinate and support LAAC meetings, ensuring members have clear information and questions.
  • Monitor fidelity and ensure every school participates meaningfully.
  • Share back "what we heard" after key listening moments so trust grows.
Board members
  • Understand purpose, timeline, and connections to district goals.
  • Ask questions that keep the focus on learner experience and evidence.
  • Use Phase 1 findings and LAAC input as context for policy and resource decisions.
  • Support public messaging that explains why Phase 1 comes before redesign decisions.
School and Classroom Roles
School and classroom level
Principals and leadership teams
  • Organize Phase 1 work at the school level and set clear expectations for staff.
  • Lead evidence review conversations and self assessment sessions.
  • Coordinate student, family, and community input at the school level.
  • Draft, refine, and finalize the School Phase 1 Summary Profile.
  • Nominate or support representatives to serve on the LAAC.
  • Embed work into PLCs and existing rhythms.
Teachers and staff
  • Provide evidence that shows what learning and assessment look like.
  • Participate honestly in surveys and self assessment conversations.
  • Suggest realistic priorities based on daily experience.
  • If on the LAAC, share patterns and listen for district patterns.
  • Help define what "good evidence" looks like so expectations are fair.
Students and families
  • Share experiences of learning, communication, and support.
  • Lift up moments when the system works and when it does not.
  • Help confirm or challenge adult interpretations of data.
  • As LAAC members, bring forward questions and ideas from peers.
Local Accountability Advisory Council (LAAC) in Phase 1
Cross stakeholder council
Purpose
The LAAC provides real time input, feedback, and sensemaking from all stakeholder groups as the district designs and implements a local accountability model.
  • Ensure students, families, staff, community partners, and the board have a structured voice.
  • Review Phase 1 tools and findings to see whether they match lived experience.
  • Identify blind spots, equity concerns, and opportunities.
  • Bridge district level work and school and community conversations.
  • Strengthen trust by making feedback visible and followed by clear action.
Expectations in Phase 1
  • The LAAC meets at least two to three times during Phase 1.
  • Members receive clear, plain language materials before each meeting.
  • Each meeting includes questions, small group discussion, and recommendations.
  • District leaders report back on how recommendations were used.
  • The LAAC helps define what will be public-facing and understandable.

7. Online Checklists and Fidelity Monitoring
Interactive checklists leaders and LAAC members can use online. Also included in the Print Pack for paper use.
School Level Checklist
For principals and school teams
  • We named our entry point and explained it to staff in plain language.
  • We engaged a representative team for Phase 1 including teachers and staff.
  • We collected evidence from classrooms, not only from dashboards.
  • We completed the Readiness Rubric using evidence and documented our reasoning.
  • We gathered at least one student input and one family input and recorded themes.
  • We created a School Phase 1 Summary Profile and shared it to invite questions.
  • We identified three to five priorities for Phase 2 and shared them with our LAAC representatives.
District Level Checklist
For central office teams
  • We clarified the entry point and published evidence anchors with examples.
  • Every school completed Phase 1 activities and submitted artifacts, with support provided where needed.
  • We checked for consistency in how schools applied the Readiness Rubric and clarified when needed.
  • We created a District Phase 1 Summary that reflects reality, not only goals.
  • We aligned Phase 1 findings to the Six Pillars, Continuum, and DDIER in at least one visual.
  • We communicated next steps to board, staff, and community, including LAAC recommendations.
  • We stored Phase 1 artifacts and summaries with clear naming conventions.
  • We published at least one "what we heard" summary.

Tip
If your micro checklist cannot be completed honestly, do not hide it. That is exactly what Phase 1 is designed to reveal.
Leadership Huddle. 20 Minutes to Keep Phase 1 on Track
A repeatable huddle structure principals and district leads can use weekly or biweekly during Phase 1. Tight, clear, and grounded in evidence.
Huddle Agenda
20 minutes
1) Where are we in the process
  • Name the current step and the next step.
  • Confirm what is due by when and who owns it.
2) What evidence did we gather this week
  • One artifact. One sentence on what it shows.
  • One insight. One constraint. One question.
3) What are we hearing from stakeholders
  • One student pattern and one family pattern.
  • What did we share back, and how quickly.
4) What needs district support
  • Time, tools, clarity, coaching, or communication support.
  • Assign an owner and a response date.
Non-negotiable huddle output
A one paragraph huddle note that captures evidence gathered, stakeholder signals, and next actions. Store it in the Phase 1 folder with date and school name.
Huddle Prompts
Use as-is
  • What is one thing we assumed that the evidence is challenging.
  • Where are students able to show thinking and application, and where are they blocked.
  • What does our evidence say about equity of opportunity across groups or courses.
  • What is one change we can pilot now that does not require Phase 2 design.
  • What do we need to clarify publicly so trust grows.
LAAC integration
Add a 2 minute LAAC check. What question should we bring to the next LAAC meeting, and what do we need to share back from the last one.
Watch for this drift
If the huddle turns into a status meeting with no evidence, stop. Phase 1 is an evidence and sensemaking cycle, not a task checklist.
