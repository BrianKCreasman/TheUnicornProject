Phase 2 is the architecture phase. Phase 1 defines success and surfaces current conditions. Phase 2 builds the structural system that can carry that definition into real decisions, routines, evidence, and reporting without collapsing back into compliance.

Most redesign efforts fail here because districts jump from vision to tool-building. Phase 2 prevents that failure by forcing the district to do disciplined system design: defining claims, mapping evidence logic, setting decision rules, clarifying governance, and designing how transparency will work for real people.

Phase 2 outcome in one sentence: A coherent, defensible accountability architecture that translates local values into usable decision rules, credible evidence expectations, and transparent reporting routines.

Design (DDIER) in Phase 2

Phase 2 is still in Design, but the work shifts from readiness scanning to architecture definition. Design here means: writing the system’s claims, defining evidence requirements, setting decision rules, building governance routines, and preventing predictable failure modes like proxy collapse and compliance theater.

Local Accountability Principles Activated in Phase 2

In Phase 2, principles stop being ideals and become system constraints. The architecture must actively protect them. If the design does not protect the principles, pressure will.

  • Systemic Coherence and Outcome Alignment: the architecture keeps outcomes, evidence, and reporting in one coherent story.
  • Shared Ownership and Community Co-Creation: design choices are visible, explainable, and shaped by stakeholder feedback loops.
  • Multiple Measures and Balanced Evidence: the system prevents a single score from becoming the definition of readiness.
  • Evidence Quality and Technical Integrity: evidence must be credible enough for the decisions it will support.
  • Equity and Opportunity to Learn: interpretation includes access and supports, not just outcomes.
  • Transparency and Interpretability: reporting is designed for understanding and action, not labeling.
  • Continuous Improvement and Learning Culture: the system is built for iteration, not verdicts.

Phase 2 Steps. What Leaders Actually Do

Phase 2 works when it is treated like system engineering. The goal is not to write more narrative. The goal is to make the accountability system operational: clear claims, clear evidence, clear decision rules, clear routines, clear reporting.

Step 1: Translate Success Into System Claims

  • Convert the Portrait of a Learner and Growth-Mastery-Readiness definitions into explicit claims the system will make about student success.
  • Write claims in plain language so stakeholders can repeat them without jargon.
  • Identify which claims require multiple evidence sources and which can be supported by existing measures.
  • Define what the system will not claim, to prevent overreach and misinterpretation.
  • Align claims to decisions: what will leaders actually do differently when evidence changes.
Pillar 1: Purpose and Logic Model DDIER: Design Principle: Transparency and Interpretability

Step 2: Build the Evidence Logic Map (Evidence Map 2.0)

  • Link each claim to a set of defensible evidence sources (student work, performance tasks, portfolios, assessments, surveys, and academic indicators as appropriate).
  • Flag evidence that is high-stakes but low-quality and cannot support major decisions yet.
  • Define minimum evidence expectations across grade spans to prevent “random acts of evidence.”
  • Design for growth over time so the system can tell a learning story, not just label a moment.
  • Identify evidence gaps that Phase 3 must build (tasks, rubrics, calibration routines, portfolio structures).
Continuum: Demonstrate Student Success Pillar 4: Data Validation and Utilization Principle: Multiple Measures Principle: Evidence Quality

Step 3: Write Decision Rules (Use, Weight, and Guardrails)

  • Define what evidence can be used for which decisions (instructional support, grading, placement, interventions, reporting, accountability).
  • Set “no single measure” rules so one number cannot override the full story of learning.
  • Define what happens when evidence conflicts. The system should trigger inquiry, not blame.
  • Define when evidence is informational versus consequential. High-stakes use requires higher quality.
  • Publish simple guidance so schools are consistent and the community sees predictable logic.
DDIER: Design Pillar 2: Governance and Leadership Principle: Evidence Quality Principle: Transparency

Step 4: Build Governance and Cadence

  • Clarify roles: who owns evidence quality, who interprets evidence, who makes which decisions, and who monitors drift.
  • Define a consistent cadence: evidence reviews, calibration cycles, stakeholder feedback loops, and reporting windows.
  • Create a decision log routine: what changed, why it changed, and what evidence informed the change.
  • Design escalation paths: when a school is stuck, what support gets triggered and how quickly.
  • Protect coherence by stopping low-value work that competes with evidence integrity.
Pillar 2: Governance and Leadership Pillar 3: Community Engagement and Advocacy Principle: Continuous Improvement

Step 5: Design the Reporting Story (Transparency by Design)

  • Design reporting so stakeholders can understand what evidence means and what actions follow.
  • Organize reporting around Growth, Mastery, and Readiness, not around one composite score.
  • Build explainers: what we measure, why it matters, what strong evidence looks like, and how to improve.
  • Separate “public understanding” reports from “internal improvement” reports so each stays usable.
  • Define what will be published, how often, and how questions will be answered without defensiveness.
Pillar 6: Reporting and Transparency Principle: Transparency and Interpretability Principle: Multiple Measures

Step 6: Phase 3 Build List and Pilot Logic

  • Convert evidence gaps into a prioritized build list (tasks, rubrics, calibration routines, portfolio structures, dashboards).
  • Define the pilot logic: where to start, what to collect, what “good enough” looks like, and how expansion decisions will be made.
  • Define minimum technical requirements: rubrics must be usable, calibratable, and interpretable.
  • Define professional learning priorities tied directly to evidence quality and consistency.
  • Publish a Phase 2 to Phase 3 transition brief so the system feels predictable and coherent.

Architecture is how the system behaves under pressure. When the architecture is weak, accountability defaults to whatever is easiest to measure, easiest to compare, and easiest to defend. Phase 2 designs the system so it can hold a learning-centered story without collapsing into compliance.

Six Pillars as Load-Bearing Supports

  • Pillar 1: keeps outcomes and claims explicit so the system knows what it is trying to prove.
  • Pillar 2: prevents responsibility drift by clarifying who owns evidence, decisions, and integrity.
  • Pillar 3: prevents insider-only accountability by building real feedback loops and public understanding.
  • Pillar 4: protects technical credibility so evidence can support the decisions people want to make.
  • Pillar 5: keeps the system learning, studying impact, and correcting itself as it scales.
  • Pillar 6: turns evidence into understandable reporting that supports action, not labeling.
Pillars: 1-6 Principle: Systemic Coherence

Continuum Flow (From Definition to Demonstration)

  • Define success with the community in observable terms.
  • Design learning experiences that can produce evidence of that success.
  • Collect evidence that is meaningful and improves instruction.
  • Interpret evidence with shared routines and shared language.
  • Respond through supports and improvements, not just labels.
  • Demonstrate student success publicly with clarity and integrity.
Continuum: Define -> Demonstrate Student Success Principle: Transparency

DDIER as the Operating Discipline

  • Design: clarify claims, evidence requirements, decision rules, and guardrails.
  • Develop: build tools that match the design (rubrics, templates, calibration, reporting prototypes).
  • Implement: pilot with real work, real constraints, and clear integrity checks.
  • Evaluate: study coherence, workload, interpretability, validity, and equity signals.
  • Revise: adjust based on evidence and learning, not politics or convenience.
Design anchor: The purpose of DDIER is to keep the system learning. If the system cannot revise, it will calcify.

Designing Evidence, Decision Rules, and Integrity Protections

Evidence and Decision Rules That Prevent Drift

Six Pillars
Local Accountability Continuum
DDIER Cycle
Local Accountability Principles

Evidence design is not neutral. It determines what becomes visible, what stays hidden, and what people believe is “real.” Phase 2 protects against predictable failure modes by designing evidence expectations and decision rules that are both meaningful and defensible.

Evidence Quality Requirements

  • Evidence must be tied to a clear claim and a clear definition of success.
  • Evidence must be interpretable by humans, not just stored in systems.
  • Rubrics must be usable and capable of being calibrated across classrooms and schools.
  • Portfolio evidence must show growth over time, not just “best work.”
  • When evidence is weak, the response is better tasks and better routines, not blame.
Pillar 4: Data Validation and Utilization Principle: Evidence Quality

Decision Rule Guardrails

  • No single measure can define readiness. Balanced evidence is required for high-stakes conclusions.
  • Conflicting evidence triggers inquiry and support, not labeling.
  • Evidence use matches evidence strength. Higher stakes require higher technical integrity.
  • Public reporting must be designed for understanding and action, not sorting and ranking.
  • Stakeholders have access to clear explanations of what evidence means and how improvement happens.
Pillar 6: Reporting and Transparency Principle: Transparency Principle: Multiple Measures

Best Practices That Protect Integrity

  • Build calibration into the calendar. Consistency is not an optional add-on.
  • Publish “how to interpret this” guides alongside any report shared publicly.
  • Use decision logs so stakeholders can see what changed and why.
  • Design reporting to drive support and improvement, not public judgment.
  • Keep the system small enough to run well. Coherence beats clutter every time.
Design check: If you cannot explain what evidence supports a claim and what decisions follow, the system is not ready to be used as accountability.

Stakeholder Engagement, Empowerment, and Reciprocity in Phase 2

In Phase 2, stakeholder roles become operational. People do not just give input. They help build a system they can understand, they can trust, and they can use. Reciprocity means each group has responsibility and benefit, not just participation.

School and District Leaders

  • Own coherence. Prevent tool-building from outrunning clarity.
  • Define decision rules and protect evidence integrity under pressure.
  • Build cadence: evidence reviews, calibration cycles, and decision logs.
  • Stop low-value requirements that create noise and compliance theater.
Pillar 2: Governance and Leadership Principle: Systemic Coherence

Teachers and School Staff

  • Test evidence expectations against real tasks and real student work.
  • Help define what “good evidence” looks like using exemplars and anchor work.
  • Participate in calibration so interpretation is consistent and fair.
  • Identify what support is needed so quality is realistic, not aspirational.
Pillar 4: Data Validation and Utilization Principle: Evidence Quality

Parents and Guardians

  • Stress-test interpretability: can families understand what reports mean and what to do next.
  • Help refine public language so transparency is real, not performative.
  • Provide feedback on what would build trust in evidence beyond test scores.
  • Help identify where reporting could unintentionally confuse or mislead.
Pillar 6: Reporting and Transparency Principle: Transparency

Students

  • Help define what strong evidence looks like in real work, not just adult language.
  • Test reflection prompts and portfolio structures for authenticity.
  • Identify where the system still misses strengths, interests, and readiness evidence.
  • Help make the reporting story human, not clinical.
Principle: Shared Ownership Principle: Multiple Measures

Community and Partners

  • Validate readiness definitions against local workforce, civic, and community expectations.
  • Offer real contexts that can become performance tasks and evidence opportunities.
  • Help review whether reporting supports understanding and improvement.
  • Act as integrity partners by asking whether design choices protect equity and opportunity.
Pillar 3: Community Engagement and Advocacy Principle: Equity and Opportunity

Best Practices Across Stakeholders

  • Publish decision logs and “you said, we did” summaries so influence is visible.
  • Review real student work together so evidence drives design, not preferences.
  • Use plain language and examples. If people cannot interpret it, it will not sustain.
  • Design supports and improvement actions alongside any evidence conversation.
Why this matters: If Phase 2 is not co-built and interpretable, it will be seen as another central office initiative. When Phase 2 is done well, the system feels clearer, lighter, and more trustworthy.

Practice in Action: Phase 2 in Fleming County Schools

In Fleming County Schools, Phase 2 is where the district protects coherence across the Portrait of a Learner, BRIDGE Performance Indicators, Next-Generation Readiness Assessments, Measures of Quality, and BPI Websites. The goal is not to add layers. The goal is to design how these pieces function together as one system.

Architecture Choices that Protect Learning

  • Evidence expectations are defined before dashboards are finalized, so reporting reflects learning instead of driving it.
  • Decision rules prevent “score takeover” by requiring balanced evidence for readiness claims.
  • Evidence quality is treated as improvable through better tasks, better rubrics, and calibration cycles.
  • Leadership cadence focuses on evidence interpretation and support actions, not compliance checks.
Pillar 2: Governance and Leadership Principle: Systemic Coherence

What Phase 2 Looks Like to Stakeholders

  • Students see clearer expectations for what counts as evidence and have structures to curate it over time.
  • Teachers have clearer guidance on how to design tasks that produce meaningful evidence.
  • Families see reporting that explains learning, not just results.
  • Board members receive information that supports governance focused on learning and improvement.
