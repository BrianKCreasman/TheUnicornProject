Phase 2: Designing the Vision: Crafting the Architecture of a Local Accountability System

Big Idea

Phase 2 is the architecture phase. Phase 1 tells the truth about current conditions and readiness. Phase 2 turns that truth into a coherent design that a district can actually run. This is where the system becomes explicit about what it will measure, what evidence will count, how evidence will be produced in classrooms, and how the public story of learning will be reported without collapsing into a single score.

The goal is not to write a vision statement that sounds good. The goal is to build a design that can survive real-world use: leaders can explain it, teachers can implement it, students can demonstrate learning through it, and families can understand it. Phase 2 is where the district makes the hard decisions about constructs, evidence quality, decision rules, governance, and transparency.

Phase 2 outcome in one sentence: A district-ready accountability architecture that links outcomes to evidence, protects technical integrity, and defines how learning will be demonstrated, interpreted, and reported.

Design (DDIER) in Phase 2

Phase 2 is still in Design, but it is a different kind of design work than Phase 1. Phase 1 defines success and identifies gaps. Phase 2 specifies the system blueprint: claims, evidence requirements, measurement approaches, scoring rules, quality controls, and reporting structures. If Phase 2 stays conceptual, later phases become unstable.

Local Accountability Principles Protected in Phase 2

Phase 2 is where principles become non-negotiable design constraints. If the design violates the principles, implementation will become compliance, and reporting will become confusing. Leaders should name these principles early and reference them as decision rules throughout the phase.

  • Systemic Coherence and Outcome Alignment: outcomes drive measures, measures drive routines, and routines produce evidence that supports decisions.
  • Shared Ownership and Community Co-Creation: stakeholders do not just approve the design. They help shape what counts as credible evidence.
  • Multiple Measures and Balanced Evidence: the architecture prevents over-weighting any single measure, including benchmarks and state tests.
  • Evidence Quality and Technical Integrity: the system defines minimum quality expectations for tasks, rubrics, scoring, and interpretation.
  • Equity and Opportunity to Learn: evidence is interpreted alongside access to strong instruction, supports, and meaningful learning opportunities.
  • Transparency and Interpretability: the design explains what evidence means in plain language and shows how improvement is possible.
  • Continuous Improvement and Learning Culture: the architecture includes calibration cycles, revision rules, and learning loops, not just outcomes.

Phase 2 Steps. What Leaders Actually Do

Phase 2 works when it is treated like system engineering, not a committee write-up. The goal is to create a blueprint that teachers can execute, leaders can monitor, and the community can interpret. Each step below ends with a tangible product. If the product is missing, the system is not yet designed.

Step 1: Convert the Portrait and Priority Standards into System Claims

  • Write a small set of district claims that the system will be accountable for (Growth, Mastery, Readiness, and key durable skills).
  • Clarify what each claim means in observable terms across grade spans. Avoid vague traits that cannot be evidenced.
  • Separate outcomes (what students can do) from inputs (what adults provide). Both matter, but they are measured differently.
  • Define the decisions each claim must support (instructional next steps, student support, reporting, governance monitoring).
  • Identify where state requirements must be included and where local measures extend the story of learning.

Step 2: Build the Evidence Pathways (What Counts, Where It Comes From, How Often)

  • Create an evidence blueprint that lists evidence sources for each claim: performance tasks, portfolios, common assessments, classroom evidence, and required tests.
  • Define frequency bands (weekly, unit-level, quarterly, annual) so evidence is not accidental or uneven across schools.
  • Specify what evidence must show: product, process, and reflection. Keep it simple and consistent.
  • Identify the minimum evidence set that makes the system credible without overwhelming classrooms.
  • Publish an evidence menu that teachers can use to design tasks that naturally produce credible artifacts.

Step 3: Define Evidence Quality Rules (Rubrics, Scoring, and Appropriate Use)

  • Write minimum quality expectations for tasks and rubrics so evidence is defensible and comparable across classrooms.
  • Define scoring approach by evidence type: teacher rubric scoring, calibrated team scoring, sampling, or external review.
  • Establish anchor examples for each rubric level so interpretation is shared, not personal.
  • Define appropriate use rules. Name what evidence can support grading, placement, interventions, and public reporting.
  • Create a calibration routine and schedule. If scoring is not calibrated, the evidence will not hold up.

Step 4: Define Governance, Roles, and Decision Rules

  • Define who owns which parts of the system: board monitoring, superintendent oversight, district design teams, school leadership routines, teacher team implementation.
  • Create a decision log structure that records what was decided, why, and what evidence informed the decision.
  • Define escalation rules when evidence shows risk (what triggers support, what triggers redesign, what triggers additional validation).
  • Clarify data governance and privacy boundaries for portfolios, dashboards, and public reporting.
  • Publish a one-page roles and responsibilities map so the architecture is operational, not assumed.
Pillar 2: Governance and Leadership Pillar 3: Community Engagement and Advocacy Principle: Shared Ownership and Co-Creation Principle: Transparency and Interpretability

Step 5: Design the Reporting Story (What the Community Will See and Understand)

  • Define how Growth, Mastery, and Readiness will be communicated together so the story stays balanced.
  • Design reporting views for different audiences: families, board, staff, students, and community partners.
  • Include plain-language interpretation guides and examples of what improvement looks like.
  • Specify how qualitative evidence (portfolios, performance artifacts) will be curated and sampled for transparency without overwhelm.
  • Write a reporting cadence and release plan. Predictability builds trust more than marketing.

Step 6: Build the Phase 3 Development Backlog (Tools and Supports)

  • Convert the architecture into a build list: rubric templates, task design tools, portfolio structures, dashboards, and reporting templates.
  • Define minimum viable versions for pilots. Do not wait for perfect before starting validation.
  • Write professional learning priorities that match the architecture: assessment literacy, task design, calibration, and evidence use.
  • Define pilot selection rules and expansion criteria tied to evidence quality and implementation readiness.
  • Create a Phase 2 to Phase 3 transition brief that explains what is built, what is tested, and how revisions will occur.

Tools, Resources, and Supports for Phase 2

Phase 2 moves faster and with fewer surprises when leaders use shared templates and decision structures. These tools keep the design grounded in evidence quality, interpretability, and system coherence.

Claims and Constructs Template

A one-page template that defines the district claims (Growth, Mastery, Readiness, durable skills), what each means, and what decisions each claim must support.

Evidence Pathways Blueprint

A matrix that links each claim to evidence sources, frequency, scoring approach, and minimum quality expectations.

Rubric and Anchor Set Starter Kit

A common rubric structure with anchor examples and scoring notes that allow calibration across teams and schools.

Decision Rules and Appropriate Use Guide

A plain-language guide that states what evidence can support which decisions, and what evidence should never be over-weighted.

Reporting Prototype Pack

Draft dashboard views, family-friendly reports, board briefs, and interpretation guides that explain what evidence means and how improvement happens.

Calibration Cadence Protocol

A recurring agenda and sampling plan for calibration cycles, including how to collect artifacts, norm scoring, document learning, and adjust rubrics.

Coalition Membership (Design Roles)

From Vision to a Practical Theory of Action

A theory of action is the bridge between inspiration and implementation. It explains how the district believes student outcomes will improve, and what evidence will show progress along the way. In Phase 2, the theory of action becomes specific: what adults will do, what students will experience, and what evidence will be collected to confirm the system is working.

Inputs

  • Portrait of a Learner and Growth-Mastery-Readiness definitions.
  • Priority standards and coherent instructional resources.
  • Common rubrics, exemplars, and portfolio structures.
  • Collaboration time and calibration routines.

Key Activities

  • Designing performance tasks and vibrant learning experiences that produce authentic evidence.
  • Scoring with shared rubrics and conducting calibration cycles.
  • Curating student portfolios with reflection and goal-setting.
  • Reviewing evidence in leadership routines and using it for improvement decisions.

Outputs

  • Student work artifacts aligned to claims and standards.
  • Scored rubrics with anchors that support consistent interpretation.
  • Portfolio samples that show growth over time.
  • Dashboards and reports that translate evidence into understanding.

Outcomes

  • More coherent learning experiences across classrooms and schools.
  • Clearer evidence of mastery and durable skills through authentic tasks.
  • Improved trust because the public story matches classroom reality.
  • A system that can validate progress and improve continuously.

Phase 2 becomes visible when leaders can point to specific evidence pathways and say, "This is how we will know." It is also visible when teachers can design lessons and assessments that naturally produce the evidence the system needs. When Phase 2 is strong, Phase 3 is not a scramble. Evidence, Guiding Questions, and Reflection for Phase 2

Phase 2 needs visible proof that the architecture is coherent and usable. Evidence shows what has been designed. Guiding questions protect clarity and scope. Reflection keeps the system learning while it is being built. These prompts are designed to be used in leadership meetings, design team sessions, and stakeholder updates.

Evidence of Efficacy

  • A written set of district claims and an evidence blueprint that links each claim to evidence sources and frequency.
  • Common rubric structures with anchor examples that support consistent interpretation across schools.
  • Documented appropriate use rules and decision rules for key decisions and reporting.
  • A reporting prototype that shows Growth, Mastery, and Readiness together with plain-language interpretation.
  • A published governance and calibration cadence that shows how evidence quality will be protected over time.

Guiding Questions

  • What evidence pathway is still ambiguous, and what is the simplest clarification that would make it usable.
  • Where are we at risk of over-weighting a single measure again, even unintentionally.
  • Do our scoring and calibration plans match the stakes of the decisions we want to make.
  • Can a family interpret a sample report and name what actions it suggests.
  • Where do opportunity-to-learn conditions need to be measured alongside outcomes so interpretation stays fair.

Reflection

  • As a district leader, what part of the architecture is most at risk of becoming confusing in practice, and how will you simplify it.
  • As a principal, what routines will you protect to keep evidence production consistent and real (planning, calibration, evidence reviews).
  • As a teacher, what task would best demonstrate readiness that a score cannot show, and what rubric would make it interpretable.
  • As a family member, what would make reporting feel honest and understandable, and what would make it feel like labels again.
  • What is one design decision we should revise now, before Phase 3 builds it into tools.

Stakeholder Engagement, Empowerment, and Reciprocity in Phase 2

Phase 2 is where engagement becomes influence. Stakeholders help decide what counts as evidence, what quality looks like, and how the public story will be told. Reciprocity is critical here: the system asks stakeholders to trust new measures, so the system must provide clarity, access, and understandable reporting in return.

School and District Leaders

  • Protect scope and coherence by requiring every design decision to link back to claims and evidence pathways.
  • Publish decision logs and architecture drafts so stakeholders can see the design evolving.
  • Define governance and escalation rules that make support predictable when evidence shows risk.
  • Commit to calibration and revision cycles so evidence stays credible over time.

Teachers and School Staff

  • Test evidence pathways using real units and tasks to confirm feasibility and clarity.
  • Help build anchor examples and scoring notes that make rubric levels meaningful.
  • Identify professional learning needs tied directly to the architecture (task design, calibration, evidence use).
  • Support students in reflection and portfolio curation so evidence is authentic, not staged.

Parents and Guardians

  • Review reporting prototypes and interpretation guides for clarity and usefulness.
  • Help define what would build trust in portfolios and performance evidence.
  • Provide feedback on what information supports action at home, not confusion.
  • Participate in feedback loops and receive clear updates on what changed because of input.

Best Practices Across Stakeholders

  • Prototype with real work. Do not design only in abstracts.
  • Publish changes made from feedback so trust grows through evidence.
  • Keep the evidence set minimal and meaningful. Protect teachers from overload.
  • Use plain language. If it cannot be explained, it cannot be sustained.
Why this matters: If stakeholders help design the evidence and reporting rules, they are far more likely to trust the story the system tells later. That trust becomes implementation capacity.

Practice in Action: Phase 2 in Fleming County Schools

In Fleming County Schools, Phase 2 is where the Measures of Quality, BRIDGE outcomes, Next-Generation Readiness Assessments, and BPI Websites are intentionally aligned. The design work clarifies what evidence will be produced in classrooms, how it will be curated by students, and how leaders will interpret it without reducing learning to a single number. Phase 2 protects coherence by ensuring the evidence system matches the district's definition of success.

Evidence Pathways through NxGRAs and Portfolios

  • Next-Generation Readiness Assessments are designed to produce evidence of both standards mastery and durable skills.
  • BPI Websites act as the portfolio structure where students curate artifacts, reflections, and growth across time.
  • Evidence expectations are clarified so students know what to show and why it matters.
  • Teacher teams use rubrics and examples to keep evidence interpretable across classrooms.

  • Transparency is built through predictable updates and plain-language interpretation guides.

Design Choices that Protect Coherence

  • Evidence is defined first, then tools are built to support the evidence system, not the other way around.
  • Rubrics and calibration routines are treated as system infrastructure, not optional add-ons.
  • Student agency is protected by requiring authentic options for demonstrating learning and readiness.
