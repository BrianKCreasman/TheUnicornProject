Phase 1 on a Page. Laying the Groundwork
Framework. Current Conditions and Readiness Framework. Evidence and Learning Loop Framework. Phase 1 Key Outcome
Goal. Build an honest picture of how learning and assessment feel today, so the local accountability model is grounded in reality, not guesses.
In plain language
  • We tell the truth about what school feels like right now for students, families, and staff.
  • We gather real examples of learning and assessment, not only test scores and rankings.
  • We create a Local Accountability Advisory Council so every stakeholder group has a seat at the table.
  • We decide, together, where we are ready to grow and where we need to build foundations first.
By the end of Phase 1, we have:
  • A one page summary for each school that captures strengths, challenges, and readiness.
  • A district wide picture of where we are starting from that the board and community can see.
  • Three to five clear priorities that will drive Phase 2 design and decisions.
  • A functioning Local Accountability Advisory Council that understands its role and next steps.
Who needs to understand this
  • Board members and district leaders who set direction and expectations.
  • Principals and teacher leaders who organize the work and gather evidence.
  • Staff, families, community partners, and students who will live the changes.
Most people only need this view. The detailed steps, checklists, and LAAC tools are for the teams actually running Phase 1.
From a student perspective
  • I know that adults are asking honest questions about what school feels like for students, not only about test scores.
  • I have at least one way to share my voice in this process such as a survey, panel, conversation, or reflection.
  • I see or hear a summary of what students are saying, not just questions going into a black box.
Student example
Eighth grade students are invited to a lunch conversation where the principal asks, "When do you feel like your learning really matters" and "What makes it hard to show what you really know". Notes from the conversation are shared with the Local Accountability Advisory Council.
From a teacher perspective
  • I understand why we are doing Phase 1 and how it connects to my classroom practice.
  • I have helped select or provide sample units, student work, or assessments that show what learning looks like in my classes.
  • I have been part of at least one conversation about what our current reality really looks like for students and teachers.
Teacher example
In a PLC, teachers bring recent assessment tasks and student work. The group looks at which tasks allow students to demonstrate thinking and which ones only ask for recall. That conversation shapes what goes into the current conditions scan.
From a staff member perspective
  • I understand that Phase 1 is about improving student experience, not blaming staff members.
  • I have at least one way to share what I see and experience in my role such as bus, cafeteria, front office, or support services.
  • I know where to find basic Phase 1 information if families ask questions at the front desk or on the phone.
Staff example
Office staff collect common parent questions and concerns for one month. Those patterns become part of the school summary under family communication and support.
From a school leader perspective
  • I can explain Phase 1 in two or three sentences to any staff member, student, or parent.
  • I have a simple schedule for how my school will collect evidence, listen to stakeholders, and complete the summary.
  • I know how Phase 1 connects to the Six Pillars, the Continuum, and DDIER for our district.
School leader example
During a faculty meeting, the principal shows a one slide visual of the Phase 1 steps and explains which parts will happen in staff meetings, which in PLCs, and which through student and family input.
From a district leader perspective
  • We have clearly communicated expectations and supports for schools during Phase 1.
  • We have a plan for how we will synthesize school level findings into a district story for the board and community.
  • We can name how Phase 1 findings will influence concrete Phase 2 design decisions.
District example
The accountability director drafts a two page Phase 1 overview and reviews it with the Local Accountability Advisory Council. Their feedback helps refine language before it goes to principals and the board.
From a board member perspective
  • I have seen at least one short overview of Phase 1 goals and how it fits into the long range accountability work.
  • I understand that Phase 1 is about learning how the system is working, not evaluating individual teachers.
  • I know when and how I will see a summary of Phase 1 findings at the board table.
Board example
At a work session, district leaders use three slides to explain Phase 1, the role of the Local Accountability Advisory Council, and how board members will eventually see school and district summaries.
From a parent or guardian perspective
  • I have heard in simple language what the district is doing in Phase 1 and why it matters for my child.
  • I have at least one easy option to share my experience, such as a survey or listening session.
  • I believe my feedback will be used to shape what happens next, not just stored in a report.
Parent example
The school sends a brief family newsletter with two questions about communication and one about grading. Results are shared back to families and discussed at the next Local Accountability Advisory Council meeting.
From a community member perspective
  • I understand that the district is building a local accountability model that goes beyond test scores.
  • I know how local businesses, organizations, or partners can share input about readiness and real world skills.
  • I have seen at least one communication that connects this work to the community future and workforce needs.
Community example
A local employer sits on the Local Accountability Advisory Council and shares how they currently hire, train, and support graduates from the district. That feedback influences how the district talks about readiness.
The Three Big Moves in Phase 1
Instead of many separate tasks, think of Phase 1 as three big moves that everyone can name and remember.
Big Move 1
Launch the Story and Form the Council
  • Explain why the district is moving toward local accountability.
  • Connect Phase 1 to student experience, not just compliance or test scores.
  • Form the Local Accountability Advisory Council with representation from each stakeholder group.
Output. A shared message and a functioning LAAC that will walk with the district through all phases.
Big Move 2
Look at Reality Together
  • Gather real examples of teaching, learning, and assessment across schools.
  • Listen to students, staff, and families about their experience.
  • Use a simple rubric to rate readiness in a few key areas.
Output. A clear, honest picture of strengths and gaps in each school that the LAAC can review and respond to.
Big Move 3
Choose Priorities and Next Steps
  • Summarize Phase 1 findings into a one page school profile.
  • Identify three to five focus areas for Phase 2 design work.
  • Combine school profiles into a district wide starting picture and preview it with the LAAC.
Output. School and district summaries that point Phase 2 in the right direction, validated by the LAAC.

1. Orientation to Phase 1
Why Phase 1 exists, what success looks like, and how Smith County leaders can use this guide as a working roadmap instead of a static document.
Purpose of Phase 1
Start with reality
The purpose of Phase 1 is to help Smith County build an honest, evidence based picture of current conditions and readiness across all schools, with a Local Accountability Advisory Council that reflects every stakeholder group.
  • Describe the current learner experience with real artifacts and data.
  • Identify strengths that can be leveraged in the local accountability design.
  • Surface gaps, inequities, and pain points that must be addressed.
  • Determine readiness levels at both school and district levels.
  • Agree on a small number of priorities that will guide Phase 2 design work.
  • Establish and activate the LAAC as a standing partner throughout all phases.
District example
At Smith County High School, the leadership team realized through Phase 1 that students had many project based experiences, but very few of those projects ever became part of a formal record of learning. The Local Accountability Advisory Council highlighted this gap and recommended that Phase 2 design include a plan for performance evidence and student portfolios.
Who This Guide Is For
Primary users
This guide is written for people who will actually do the work of Phase 1 in Smith County Schools, KY.
  • Superintendent and central office leaders who frame the work and protect time for it.
  • District accountability, curriculum, and assessment leads who design tools and coach schools.
  • Principals and assistant principals who organize and lead Phase 1 in each building.
  • Teacher leaders and PLC facilitators who bring classroom evidence and insight.
  • Program directors for special education, multilingual learners, counseling, and other services.
  • Members of the Local Accountability Advisory Council who represent students, families, staff, and community.
How to use this guide
Use this guide as a living playbook. Print key sections for leadership meetings, link it in your local accountability site, and mark it up as you learn from practice. The view toggle at the top lets you move between an Everyone view and a Leader Edition. LAAC members can use the Everyone view and selected leader sections to understand the full process.
Orientation for students

For students, Phase 1 should feel like adults are genuinely listening. You are not being evaluated. The system is. Your job is to be honest about what helps you learn and what gets in the way.

  • I know who to talk to at my school if I have feedback about learning, grading, or belonging.
  • I have been asked questions like "What helps you learn best" and "When does school feel most meaningful".
  • If I serve on the LAAC, I know that my role is to bring student voice and examples, not to speak for every student alone.
Orientation for teachers

For teachers, Phase 1 is a chance to put real classroom evidence on the table. It is not another inspection. It is a way to show what is already working and to name the conditions that limit your impact.

  • I have seen examples of the kinds of artifacts the district is looking for such as units, tasks, and student work.
  • I know how my voice and evidence will be used to inform district decisions and LAAC discussions.
  • If I am a teacher representative on the LAAC, I understand I am there to share patterns, not only individual stories.
Orientation for school leaders

For principals and assistant principals, Phase 1 is the moment to align your leadership team, PLCs, and stakeholder groups around an honest look at your school. You are the bridge between system architecture and classroom reality.

  • Our leadership team has reviewed this guide together and assigned leads for each step.
  • We have decided where Phase 1 work will sit such as staff meetings, PLCs, leadership huddles, and LAAC updates.
  • We know who from our school serves on the LAAC and how we will keep them informed.
Orientation for district leaders

For district leaders, Phase 1 must be coherent across schools but not rigid. Your challenge is to balance clarity of expectations with enough flexibility for schools to show their unique context, while keeping the LAAC engaged and informed.

  • We have named non negotiables for Phase 1 and where schools can adapt tools.
  • We have identified who will coach and support principals as they implement the steps.
  • We have scheduled LAAC meetings at key points in Phase 1 for input and sensemaking.
Orientation for board members

For board members, Phase 1 is the foundation for future decisions about policy, resources, and priorities. You are not running the process, but your understanding and support signal that this is serious work.

  • I have asked district leaders how Phase 1 aligns with our current board goals and adopted plans.
  • I have clarity on when the board will see a Phase 1 summary and how LAAC feedback will be included.
Orientation for parents and guardians

For families, Phase 1 should feel like an invitation. The district is asking, "What is it like to have your child in our schools" and "How can we do better". Your lived experience matters.

  • I have received at least one simple explanation of this work from the school or district.
  • I know how to give feedback if I choose to and what will happen with that feedback.
Orientation for staff members

For support staff, Phase 1 is a chance to describe what you see in hallways, buses, offices, cafeterias, and playgrounds. You see parts of the system that others miss and your perspective is essential.

  • I have been asked about what I notice in student behavior, stress, and relationships in the spaces where I work.
Orientation for community members

For community partners, Phase 1 is the entry point into a shared story about readiness. It connects classroom learning to the skills, habits, and character that matter in your organizations and workplaces.

  • I know how to share my perspective on what graduates need to thrive beyond school.
  • If I serve on the LAAC, I understand how my voice connects to student learning and community goals.
2. Preconditions and Readiness to Launch Phase 1
A quick check for Smith County to confirm that minimum conditions are in place before asking schools and the LAAC to begin.
Minimum District Readiness
Before launch
District leaders should review these items before launching Phase 1.
  • We can explain in two or three sentences why the district is moving toward a local accountability model and how it connects to student experience.
  • We have introduced the Six Pillars, the Local Accountability Continuum, and the DDIER cycle to school leaders at a basic level.
  • We have identified a Phase 1 district lead team and clarified roles for accountability, curriculum, assessment, and school support.
  • We have designed draft templates for the Current Conditions Scan, the Readiness Rubric, and the School Phase 1 Summary Profile.
  • We have drafted a plan for how the LAAC will be formed, how members will be selected, and how often it will meet during Phase 1.
  • We have a clear plan for how schools will submit artifacts and how the district will organize and access them.
LAAC focus in this stage
The LAAC chair or co chairs are identified, and a short charter is drafted that defines the council purpose in Phase 1. even before the full membership is finalized.

3. Roles, Responsibilities, and the Local Accountability Advisory Council
Clear lanes for who does what in Phase 1 so work in Smith County is collaborative but not confusing, with the LAAC as a visible connector across stakeholder groups.
System Level Roles
District and board
Superintendent and district leaders
  • Frame the purpose of Phase 1 publicly and consistently, including why the LAAC matters.
  • Protect time for schools to do the work without feeling rushed or overloaded.
  • Provide tools, coaching, and feedback to principals and school teams.
  • Coordinate and support LAAC meetings, ensuring members have clear information and questions.
  • Monitor fidelity and ensure that every school participates meaningfully.
Board members
  • Understand the purpose, timeline, and connections to district goals.
  • Ask questions that keep the focus on student learning and readiness.
  • Use Phase 1 findings and LAAC input as context for policy and resource decisions.
LAAC focus in this stage
District leaders present a draft LAAC charter to the board, clarifying how members will be selected, how the council will function, and how its voice will be integrated into district decisions.
School and Classroom Roles
School and classroom level
Principals and leadership teams
  • Organize Phase 1 work at the school level and set clear expectations for staff.
  • Lead evidence review conversations and self assessment sessions.
  • Coordinate student, family, and community input at the school level.
  • Draft, refine, and finalize the School Phase 1 Summary Profile.
  • Nominate or support representatives from their school to serve on the LAAC.
Teachers and staff
  • Provide classroom and program level evidence that shows what learning and assessment look like.
  • Participate honestly in surveys, focus groups, and self assessment conversations.
  • Suggest realistic priorities and changes based on daily experience.
  • If serving on the LAAC, share patterns from their school and listen for patterns across the district.
Students and families
  • Share experiences of learning, communication, and support in district schools.
  • Lift up moments when the system works well and times when it does not.
  • Help confirm or challenge the way adults interpret the data.
  • As LAAC members, bring forward questions and ideas from peers and networks.
LAAC membership example
A secondary student leader, an elementary parent, a high school teacher, a middle school principal, a local employer, and a board member all serve on the LAAC along with district leaders. Each member knows how they were selected and what their responsibilities are.
Local Accountability Advisory Council (LAAC) in Phase 1
Cross stakeholder council
Purpose
The LAAC exists to provide real time input, feedback, and sensemaking from all stakeholder groups as the district designs and implements a local accountability model.
  • Ensure that students, families, staff, community partners, and the board have a structured voice.
  • Review Phase 1 tools and findings to see whether they match lived experience.
  • Help the district identify blind spots, equity concerns, and opportunities.
  • Act as a bridge between district level work and conversations in schools and the community.
Membership
  • Students from different grade levels and school types.
  • Teachers representing elementary, middle, and high schools.
  • Classified and support staff such as office, bus, or cafeteria.
  • School leaders such as principals and assistant principals.
  • District leaders from instruction, assessment, and student services.
  • Parents and guardians from different neighborhoods and backgrounds.
  • Community members and local employers.
  • One or more board members in a listening and learning role.
Expectations in Phase 1
  • The LAAC meets at least two to three times during Phase 1.
  • Members receive clear, plain language materials before each meeting.
  • Each meeting includes time for questions, small group discussion, and recommendations.
  • District leaders follow up on LAAC recommendations and report back on how they were used.

Eight steps that keep Phase 1 organized from launch through district synthesis, with the LAAC engaged throughout.
High level process overview
Use in leadership meetings
Leaders can use this map as a visual agenda so everyone knows where the work sits and what comes next.
  • Step 1. Launch and framing
  • Step 2. Form and activate the LAAC
  • Step 3. Current conditions evidence collection
  • Step 4. School self assessment and readiness rating

5. Step by Step Guidance
Detailed guidance for each of the eight steps, including realistic scenes leaders and LAAC members can recognize.
Step 1. Launch and Framing
Create a shared understanding of Phase 1 across the district.
+
Intent
Help every principal, teacher, staff member, student, and board member understand what Phase 1 is, why the district is doing it, and what will be expected of them.
Implementation sequence
  1. Develop a Phase 1 message set. District leaders draft a short narrative that connects Phase 1 to student experience, the Local Accountability Framework, and the future local accountability model.
  2. District leadership launch. Superintendent and district team use the message set in a principals meeting, including a short overview of the LAAC, and provide time for questions and concerns.
  3. School level launch. Principals adapt the message for their staff and present it in a faculty meeting, using local examples from their building. Staff see how Phase 1 fits into existing work, not as a separate initiative.
  4. Public communication. A brief family and community message uses the same core language, posted on the district site and shared through schools, and includes an invitation to learn more about the LAAC.
Sample talking points for principals
Leadership script
"We are beginning Phase 1 of our Local Accountability work. This is not a new test or a new label. It is a chance to tell the truth about how learning and assessment feel in our classrooms right now. We will collect real examples from our school, listen to students and families, and look at our data together. Our goal is simple. We want a clear picture of where we are so we can design a better system for our students, together with our Local Accountability Advisory Council."
Evidence of completion
  • Phase 1 overview shared with principals, staff, and the LAAC.
  • Staff in each school can state in simple terms what Phase 1 is about and why it matters.
  • Communication artifacts are stored in the Phase 1 shared folder and available to LAAC members.
LAAC focus in this step
LAAC members receive the same Phase 1 message set and provide feedback on clarity and language before broader public communication goes out.
Step 2. Form and Activate the Local Accountability Advisory Council (LAAC)
Put a real cross stakeholder council in place.
+
Intent
Build a council that reflects the community and has a clear purpose, role, and schedule in Phase 1 and beyond.
Implementation sequence
  1. Confirm membership categories. District leaders decide how many seats will come from each stakeholder group such as students, parents, teachers, staff, school leaders, community, and board.
  2. Invite and select members. Principals, staff, and community organizations help identify possible members. Some are invited directly, and some may express interest through an open form.
  3. Hold an orientation meeting. The district holds the first LAAC meeting to review the Phase 1 purpose, the Framework, and the council charter, and to answer questions.
  4. Set a meeting schedule. LAAC members agree on two or three meeting dates during Phase 1 that align with key milestones in the process map.
LAAC example
The district invites one student from each secondary school, one teacher from each level, a parent from each attendance area, a local employer, a faith based leader, and a board member to join the LAAC. At the first meeting, members write on sticky notes what "real accountability" should mean in their community.
Evidence of completion
  • A written LAAC charter that defines purpose, membership, and expectations.
  • A current roster of LAAC members with contact information and represented stakeholder groups.
  • A Phase 1 meeting schedule for the LAAC that aligns with the eight steps.
Step 3. Current Conditions Evidence Collection
Gather a well rounded picture of how the system is actually working.
+
Intent
Move beyond opinion and gather concrete evidence about learning, assessment, climate, and community connection.
Domains to scan
  • Instruction and Vibrant Learning experiences.
  • Assessment practices, grading, and feedback.
  • Use of data in PLCs and leadership decisions.
  • Student voice, agency, and engagement in classes.
  • School climate, belonging, and relationships.
  • Family communication and community partnership.
  • Operational conditions that support or hinder learning.
Implementation sequence
  1. Create the Current Conditions Scan Tool. District team builds a simple template with domains, guiding questions, and space for strengths, gaps, and questions.
  2. Identify existing evidence. Each school lists what data and artifacts already exist for each domain and where there are gaps.
  3. Collect targeted evidence. Schools gather missing artifacts such as sample units, student work, observation notes, and survey results.
  4. Build an evidence inventory. Each school logs each artifact with a title, date, domain, and brief description in a shared tracker.
School example
At one elementary school, the principal asks each grade level team to submit two units and six pieces of student work that show how students demonstrate understanding in reading and math. Teachers also add short reflections about where the current units feel relevant and where they feel disconnected from student interests.
LAAC focus in this step
LAAC members review the list of evidence types and suggest additional sources such as employer feedback, graduate follow up data, or student artifacts from extracurricular programs.
Step 4. School Self Assessment and Readiness Rating
Turn evidence into clear readiness levels for each school.
+
Intent
Use a shared rubric to help each school rate its readiness for local accountability work in key domains, using the evidence collected.
Sample readiness rubric slice
Domain Emerging Developing Implementing
Assessment and Multiple Measures Most evidence of learning is test based and short term. Some performance tasks and projects exist but they are not used consistently. Performance tasks, projects, and portfolios are part of normal practice in many classrooms.
Implementation sequence
  1. Introduce the rubric. District leaders explain each domain and level, with examples from real schools.
  2. School leadership team rating. Each school leadership team reviews evidence and assigns preliminary ratings, noting where evidence is thin.
  3. Teacher and staff check. Teachers and key staff provide feedback on whether ratings feel accurate based on their experience.
  4. Finalize school ratings. Ratings are finalized and recorded in the district tracker for later LAAC review.
LAAC focus in this step
LAAC members look at sample rubric ratings for a few schools to see if they align with what students, families, and community members experience. They flag any domains where the rubric language feels unclear or incomplete.
Step 5. Alignment to Pillars, Continuum, and DDIER
Connect Phase 1 work to the larger local accountability architecture.
+
Intent
Ensure that Phase 1 is not a stand alone activity, but part of the Six Pillars, the Local Accountability Continuum, and the DDIER improvement cycle.
Implementation sequence
  1. Tag evidence and ratings. District and school teams tag relevant findings to pillars such as governance, data, and reporting.
  2. Map to the Continuum. Leaders discuss where the district currently sits in the Continuum stages such as defining success, designing experiences, and demonstrating success.
  3. Connect to DDIER. Teams note where Phase 1 has already informed design, development, or evaluation decisions, and where revision will be needed.
LAAC focus in this step
LAAC members review a simple one page map that shows how Phase 1 findings sit inside the pillars and Continuum. They offer suggestions on where community communication should be strongest.
Step 6. Stakeholder Input, LAAC Review, and Sensemaking
Bring voices together around what the evidence is saying.
+
Intent
Make sure Phase 1 reflects the lived experiences of students, staff, and families, and that the LAAC has a structured role in interpreting what the evidence means.
Implementation sequence
  1. Share school level findings. Principals share a draft of their school ratings and key themes with staff and families, inviting reactions.
  2. Hold focus groups or panels. Student, teacher, and family groups provide feedback on what feels accurate and what may be missing.
  3. LAAC sensemaking. The LAAC reviews district wide patterns and asks, "If this is our story, what does it call us to do next".
Sensemaking example
In a LAAC meeting, members work in mixed groups of students, parents, staff, and leaders. Each group picks one domain such as student voice and writes three statements. "What we are encouraged by", "What worries us", and "What we hope changes in Phase 2".
Step 7. School Phase 1 Summary Profile
Synthesize findings into a concise, shareable portrait.
+
Intent
Help each school tell its Phase 1 story in a short, clear document that can guide Phase 2 design.
Implementation sequence
  1. Draft the profile. School leadership team completes each section of the School Summary Profile using Phase 1 tools and notes.
  2. Share with staff and LAAC reps. Draft profiles are shared with staff and any LAAC members connected to that school for feedback.
  3. Finalize and submit. Profiles are revised and then submitted to the district for synthesis.
LAAC focus in this step
LAAC members look at a sample of School Summary Profiles to see whether they are understandable to non educators and whether key concerns from Phase 1 show up clearly.
Step 8. District Synthesis, LAAC Recommendations, and Closure
Create the district wide picture and mark the end of Phase 1.
+
Intent
Pull together School Phase 1 Summary Profiles into a coherent district story, invite LAAC recommendations, and provide clear closure before moving to Phase 2.
Implementation sequence
  1. District synthesis. District team identifies common strengths, gaps, and readiness patterns across schools.
  2. LAAC recommendations. LAAC reviews the district synthesis and makes specific recommendations for Phase 2 design priorities.
  3. Board and community update. District leaders present a Phase 1 summary and LAAC recommendations to the board and community.
  4. Closure and preview. The district officially closes Phase 1 and previews what Phase 2 will involve for schools and stakeholders.

7. Online Checklists and Fidelity Monitoring
Interactive checklists that school and district leaders, LAAC members, and other stakeholders can use online or print as part of their Phase 1 documentation.
School Level Checklist
For principals and school teams
  • We engaged a representative leadership team for Phase 1 that included classroom teachers and staff.
  • We collected evidence from classrooms, not only from office level reports or test score dashboards.
  • We completed the Readiness Rubric using evidence rather than opinion and documented our reasoning.
  • We gathered at least one form of student voice and one form of family input and recorded key themes.
  • We created a School Phase 1 Summary Profile and shared it with staff in a way that invited questions.
  • We identified three to five priorities that will guide Phase 2 design work and shared them with our LAAC representatives.
District Level Checklist
For central office teams
  • Every school completed Phase 1 activities and submitted artifacts, with adjustments made where support was needed.
  • We checked for consistency in how schools applied the Readiness Rubric and clarified when needed.
  • We created a District Phase 1 Summary that reflects the reality of schools, not only district goals.
  • We aligned Phase 1 findings to the Six Pillars, the Continuum, and the DDIER cycle in at least one visual.
  • We communicated key Phase 1 messages and next steps to the board, staff, and community, including LAAC recommendations.
  • We stored Phase 1 artifacts and summaries in a shared, easy to access location with clear naming conventions.
Micro Checklists by Stakeholder
Perspective based
Student quick check
  • I have been asked about my experience in at least one survey, focus group, or conversation.
  • I can name at least one change adults are considering based on what students said.
Teacher quick check
  • I know where our school is in the Phase 1 process and what comes next.
  • I have contributed at least one piece of evidence or one idea that will show up in our school summary.
Parent or guardian quick check
  • I have had at least one chance to share my experience this year.
  • I can name at least one way the school has acted on family feedback.
Community member quick check
  • I have been invited to share insight about what readiness looks like after graduation.

Staff member quick check
  • I shared at least one observation about student behavior, climate, or support that leaders listened to.
Leadership Huddle Script
A turnkey panel you can drop into principal meetings, Wednesday Leadership Connection, or PLC agendas, with prompts for different stakeholders and a clear role for the LAAC.
Phase 1 Leadership Huddle
Use in 15 minute check ins
Three big questions for principals to ask their teams
  1. Where is the learner experience already strong in our school. What evidence can we point to that shows students are engaged, challenged, and supported.
  2. Where does our current system get in the way of meaningful learning. Which policies, grading practices, or routines feel misaligned with what we say we value.
  3. If we could only change one thing based on Phase 1 findings. What would have the biggest impact on student learning and sense of readiness this year.
Huddle prompts for teachers
  • If you could redesign one assessment practice at our school, what would it be and why.
  • Which recent learning experience felt most meaningful to students. What made it different.
Huddle prompts for student voice groups
  • When did you feel most proud of your learning this year. What was happening.
  • What makes it easier or harder for you to show what you really know.
Huddle prompts for family sessions
  • When does school feel most connected to your child future.
  • What is one thing you wish we would change about how we communicate learning and progress.
Three watch outs during Phase 1
  • Watch out for blaming people instead of systems. Focus on patterns and structures, not individual teachers or students.
  • Watch out for collecting data you will not use. Only gather evidence that will help you make decisions in Phase 2.
  • Watch out for trying to fix everything at once. Phase 1 is about clarity and priorities, not solving the entire system in one step.
