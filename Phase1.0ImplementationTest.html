Phase 1 on a Page. Laying the Groundwork
Framework. Current Conditions and Readiness Framework. Evidence and Learning Loop Framework. Phase 1 Key Outcome
Goal. Build an honest picture of how learning and assessment feel today, so the local accountability model is grounded in reality, not guesses.
In plain language
  • We tell the truth about what school feels like right now for students, families, and staff.
  • We gather real examples of learning and assessment, not only test scores and rankings.
  • We decide, together, where we are ready to grow and where we need to build foundations first.
By the end of Phase 1, we have:
  • A one page summary for each school that captures strengths, challenges, and readiness.
  • A district wide picture of where we are starting from.
  • Three to five clear priorities that will drive Phase 2 design and decisions.
Who needs to understand this?
  • Board members and district leaders who set direction.
  • Principals and teacher leaders who organize the work.
  • Staff, families, and community partners who will live the changes.
Most people only need this view. The detailed steps and tools are for the teams actually running Phase 1.
From a student perspective
  • I know that adults are asking honest questions about what school feels like for students.
  • I have at least one way to share my voice in this process. survey, panel, conversation, or reflection.
  • I see that when I share feedback, someone acknowledges it or explains next steps.
Example. A student panel is invited to talk about grading and feedback. The facilitator explains that this is part of Phase 1, not just a one time event.
From a teacher perspective
  • I understand why we are doing Phase 1 and how it connects to my classroom.
  • I have helped select or provide sample units, student work, or assessments that show what learning looks like.
  • I have been part of at least one conversation about what our current reality really looks like.
Example. A PLC uses part of its meeting time to examine current assessment tasks and talk about which ones truly capture student thinking.
From a staff member perspective
  • I understand that Phase 1 is about improving student experience, not blaming staff.
  • I have at least one way to share what I see and experience in my role.
  • I know where to find the basic Phase 1 information if families ask me about it.
From a school leader perspective
  • I can explain Phase 1 in two or three sentences to any staff member or parent.
  • I have a simple schedule for how my school will collect evidence, listen to stakeholders, and complete the summary.
  • I know how Phase 1 connects to the Six Pillars, the Continuum, and DDIER.
From a district leader perspective
  • We have clearly communicated expectations and supports for schools during Phase 1.
  • We have a plan for how we will synthesize school level findings into a district story for the board and community.
  • We can name how Phase 1 findings will influence Phase 2 design decisions.
From a board member perspective
  • I have seen at least one short overview of Phase 1 goals and timeline.
  • I understand that Phase 1 is about learning how the system is working, not evaluating individual teachers.
  • I know when and how I will see a summary of Phase 1 findings.
From a parent or guardian perspective
  • I have heard in simple language what the district is doing in Phase 1 and why.
  • I have at least one easy option to share my experience, such as a survey or listening session.
  • I believe my feedback will be used to shape what happens next.
From a community member perspective
  • I understand that the district is building a local accountability model that goes beyond test scores.
  • I know how local businesses, organizations, or partners can share input.
  • I have seen at least one communication that connects this work to the community future.
The Three Big Moves in Phase 1
Instead of seven separate tasks, think of Phase 1 as three moves that everyone can name and remember.
Big Move 1
Launch the Story
  • Explain why the district is moving toward local accountability.
  • Connect Phase 1 to student experience, not just compliance.
  • Set simple expectations for what schools will do in this phase.
Output. A shared message that every principal and teacher can restate in their own words.
Big Move 2
Look at Reality
  • Gather real examples of teaching, learning, and assessment.
  • Listen to students, staff, and families about their experience.
  • Use a simple rubric to rate readiness in a few key areas.
Output. A clear, honest picture of strengths and gaps in each school.
Big Move 3
Choose Priorities
  • Summarize Phase 1 findings into a one page school profile.
  • Identify three to five focus areas for Phase 2 design work.
  • Combine school profiles into a district wide starting picture.
Output. School and district summaries that point Phase 2 in the right direction.

1. Orientation to Phase 1
Why Phase 1 exists, what success looks like, and how Smith County leaders can use this guide as a working roadmap rather than a static document.
Purpose of Phase 1
Start with reality
The purpose of Phase 1 is to help Smith County build an honest, evidence based picture of current conditions and readiness across all schools. Phase 1 asks a simple question. What is the actual learner and teacher experience right now.
  • Describe the current learner experience with real artifacts and data.
  • Identify strengths that can be leveraged in the local accountability design.
  • Surface gaps, inequities, and pain points that must be addressed.
  • Determine readiness levels at both school and district levels.
  • Agree on a small number of priorities that will guide Phase 2 design work.
District Example
At Smith County High School, the leadership team realized through Phase 1 that students had many project based experiences, but very few of those projects ever became part of a formal record of learning. That insight became a central design driver in Phase 2.
Who This Guide Is For
Primary users
This guide is written for people who will actually do the work of Phase 1 in Smith County Schools, KY.
  • Superintendent and central office leaders who frame the work and protect time for it.
  • District accountability, curriculum, and assessment leads who design tools and coach schools.
  • Principals and assistant principals who organize and lead Phase 1 in each building.
  • Teacher leaders and PLC facilitators who bring classroom evidence and insight.
  • Program directors for special education, multilingual learners, counseling, and other services.
  • Student leadership teams, family advisory councils, and community partners as informed voices.
How to use this guide
Use this guide as a living playbook. Print key sections for leadership meetings, link it in your local accountability site, and mark it up as you learn from practice. The view toggle at the top lets you move between an Everyone view and a Leader Edition.
Orientation for students

For students, Phase 1 should feel like adults are genuinely listening. You are not being evaluated. The system is. Your job is to be honest about what helps you learn and what gets in the way.

  • I know who to talk to at my school if I have feedback about learning or grading.
  • I have been asked questions like "What helps you learn best" and "When does school feel most meaningful".
Orientation for teachers

For teachers, Phase 1 is a chance to put real classroom evidence on the table. It is not another inspection. It is a way to show what is already working and to name the conditions that limit your impact.

  • I have seen examples of the kinds of artifacts the district is looking for.
  • I know how my voice and evidence will be used to inform district decisions.
Orientation for school leaders

For principals and assistant principals, Phase 1 is the moment to align your leadership team, PLCs, and stakeholder groups around an honest look at your school. You are the bridge between system architecture and classroom reality.

  • Our leadership team has reviewed this guide together and assigned leads for each step.
  • We have decided where Phase 1 work will sit. staff meetings, PLCs, leadership huddles.
Orientation for district leaders

For district leaders, Phase 1 must be coherent across schools but not rigid. Your challenge is to balance clarity of expectations with enough flexibility for schools to show their unique context.

  • We have named non negotiables for Phase 1 and where schools can adapt tools.
  • We have identified who will coach and support principals as they implement the steps.
Orientation for board members

For board members, Phase 1 is the foundation for future decisions about policy, resources, and priorities. You are not running the process, but your understanding and support will signal that this is serious work.

  • I have asked district leaders how Phase 1 aligns with our current board goals.
  • I have clarity on when the board will see a Phase 1 summary.
Orientation for parents and guardians

For families, Phase 1 should feel like an invitation. The district is asking, "What is it like to have your child in our schools" and "How can we do better". Your lived experience matters.

  • I have received at least one simple explanation of this work from the school or district.
  • I know how to give feedback if I choose to.
Orientation for staff members

For support staff, Phase 1 is a chance to describe what you see in hallways, buses, offices, cafeterias, and playgrounds. You see parts of the system that others miss and your perspective is essential.

Orientation for community members

For community partners, Phase 1 is the entry point into a shared story about readiness. It connects classroom learning to the skills, habits, and character that matter in your organizations and workplaces.

2. Preconditions and Readiness to Launch Phase 1
A quick check for Smith County to confirm that minimum conditions are in place before asking schools to begin.
Minimum District Readiness
Before launch
District leaders should review these items before launching Phase 1.
  • We can explain in two or three sentences why the district is moving toward a local accountability model and how it connects to student experience.
  • We have introduced the Six Pillars, the Local Accountability Continuum, and the DDIER cycle to school leaders at a basic level.
  • We have identified a Phase 1 district lead team and clarified roles for accountability, curriculum, assessment, and school support.
  • We have created draft templates for the Current Conditions Scan, the Readiness Rubric, and the School Phase 1 Summary Profile.
  • We have a clear plan for how schools will submit artifacts and how the district will organize and access them.

3. Roles and Responsibilities
Clear lanes for who does what in Phase 1 so work in Smith County is collaborative but not confusing.
System Level Roles
District and board
Superintendent and district leaders
  • Frame the purpose of Phase 1 publicly and consistently.
  • Protect time for schools to do the work without feeling rushed or overloaded.
  • Provide tools, coaching, and feedback to principals and school teams.
  • Monitor fidelity and ensure that every school participates meaningfully.
Board members
  • Understand the purpose, timeline, and connections to district goals.
  • Ask questions that keep the focus on student learning and readiness.
  • Use Phase 1 findings as context for policy and resource decisions.
What this looks like for district leaders
  • I schedule recurring check ins with principals about Phase 1 progress.
  • I co design or review the tools schools are using so they align with the Framework language.
  • I prepare short briefs that keep the superintendent and board informed without overwhelming them.
What this looks like for board members
  • I request clear, visual summaries instead of only long narrative reports.
  • I ask "How do we know" questions that point back to evidence from classrooms and stakeholders.
  • I avoid turning Phase 1 updates into personnel evaluations.
What this looks like for community members

Community partners may serve on advisory councils, attend listening sessions, or provide insight about what readiness looks like beyond school.

School and Classroom Roles
School and classroom level
Principals and leadership teams
  • Organize Phase 1 work at the school level and set clear expectations for staff.
  • Lead evidence review conversations and self assessment sessions.
  • Coordinate student, family, and community input at the school level.
  • Draft, refine, and finalize the School Phase 1 Summary Profile.
Teachers and staff
  • Provide classroom and program level evidence that shows what learning and assessment look like.
  • Participate honestly in surveys, focus groups, and self assessment conversations.
  • Suggest realistic priorities and changes based on daily experience.
Students and families
  • Share experiences of learning, communication, and support in district schools.
  • Lift up moments when the system works well and times when it does not.
  • Help confirm or challenge the way adults interpret the data.
Role of teachers in Phase 1
  • I have selected sample units and student work that reflect the range of learners in my class, not only the strongest or weakest.
  • I have shared my concerns and hopes about grading, assessment, and student readiness in at least one structured conversation.
  • I have helped identify where our current practices already align with the Portrait of a Learner or district expectations.
Role of school leaders in Phase 1
  • I have named at least one staff member to champion Phase 1 logistics in our building.
  • I have scheduled time in existing meetings for evidence review rather than adding separate meetings whenever possible.
  • I have created a simple timeline or visual for my staff that shows the steps and what we will produce.
Role of students in Phase 1
  • I have been invited to share my experience in a way that feels safe and respectful.
  • I can name at least one change that adults are considering based on student input.
Role of parents and guardians
  • I have shared something specific that is going well for my child at school.
  • I have shared at least one suggestion or concern about learning, communication, or assessment.
Role of staff members

Staff members such as bus drivers, office staff, instructional assistants, and cafeteria workers see important patterns in student behavior, relationships, and stress. Sharing those observations helps leaders see the full picture.

5. Step by Step Guidance
Detailed guidance for each of the seven steps, including realistic scenes leaders can recognize.
Step 1. Launch and Framing
Create a shared understanding of Phase 1 across the district.
+
Intent
Help every principal, teacher, and staff member understand what Phase 1 is, why the district is doing it, and what will be expected of them.
Implementation sequence
  1. Develop a Phase 1 message set. District leaders draft a short narrative that connects Phase 1 to student experience and to the long term vision for local accountability.
  2. District leadership launch. Superintendent and district team use the message set in a principals meeting and provide time for questions and concerns.
  3. School level launch. Principals adapt the message for their staff and present it in a faculty meeting, using local examples from their building.
Sample talking points for principals
Leadership script
"We are beginning Phase 1 of our Local Accountability work. This is not a new test or a new label. It is a chance to tell the truth about how learning and assessment feel in our classrooms right now. We will collect real examples from our school, listen to students and families, and look at our data together. Our goal is simple. We want a clear picture of where we are so we can design a better system for our students."
Evidence of completion
  • Phase 1 overview shared with principals and staff.
  • Staff in each school can state in simple terms what Phase 1 is about and why it matters.
  • Communication artifacts are stored in the Phase 1 shared folder.
Step 2. Current Conditions Evidence Collection
Gather a well rounded picture of how the system is actually working.
+
Intent
Move beyond opinion and gather concrete evidence about learning, assessment, climate, and community connection.
Domains to scan
  • Instruction and Vibrant Learning experiences.
  • Assessment practices, grading, and feedback.
  • Use of data in PLCs and leadership decisions.
  • Student voice, agency, and engagement in classes.
  • School climate, belonging, and relationships.
  • Family communication and community partnership.
  • Operational conditions that support or hinder learning.
Implementation sequence
  1. Create the Current Conditions Scan Tool. District team builds a simple template with domains, guiding questions, and space for strengths, gaps, and questions.
  2. Identify existing evidence. Each school lists what data and artifacts already exist for each domain and where there are gaps.
  3. Collect targeted evidence. Schools gather missing artifacts such as sample units, student work, observation notes, and survey results.
  4. Build an evidence inventory. Each school logs each artifact with a title, date, domain, and brief description.
School Example
At one elementary school, the principal asked each grade level team to submit two units and six pieces of student work that showed how students demonstrate understanding in reading and math. Teachers also added short reflections about where the current units feel relevant and where they feel disconnected from student interests.
Step 3. School Self Assessment and Readiness Rating
Turn evidence into clear readiness levels for each school.
+
Intent
Use a shared rubric to help each school rate its readiness for local accountability work in key domains.
Sample readiness rubric slice
Domain Emerging Developing Implementing
Assessment and Multiple Measures Most evidence of learning is test based and short term. Some performance tasks and projects exist but they are not used consistently. Performance tasks, projects, and portfolios are part of normal practice in many classrooms.
Step 4. Alignment to Pillars, Continuum, and DDIER
Connect Phase 1 work to the larger Local Accountability architecture.
+
Intent
Ensure that Phase 1 is not a stand alone activity, but part of the Six Pillars, the Local Accountability Continuum, and the DDIER improvement cycle.
Step 5. Stakeholder Input and Sensemaking
Bring student, staff, and family voice into the picture of reality.
+
Intent
Make sure Phase 1 reflects the lived experiences of students, staff, and families, not only leadership perspectives.
Step 6. School Phase 1 Summary Profile
Synthesize findings into a concise, shareable portrait.
+
Intent
Help each school tell its Phase 1 story in a short, clear document that can guide Phase 2 design.
Step 7. District Synthesis and Closure
Create the district wide picture and mark the end of Phase 1.
+
Intent
Pull together School Phase 1 Summary Profiles into a coherent district story and provide clear closure before moving to Phase 2.

7. Online Checklists and Fidelity Monitoring
Interactive checklists that school and district leaders and other stakeholders can use online or print as part of their Phase 1 documentation.
School Level Checklist
For principals and school teams
  • We engaged a representative leadership team for Phase 1 that included classroom teachers.
  • We collected evidence from classrooms, not only from office level reports.
  • We completed the Readiness Rubric using evidence rather than opinion.
  • We gathered at least one form of student voice and one form of family input.
  • We created a School Phase 1 Summary Profile and shared it with staff.
  • We identified three to five priorities that will guide Phase 2 design work.
District Level Checklist
For central office teams
  • Every school completed Phase 1 activities and submitted artifacts.
  • We checked for consistency in how schools applied the Readiness Rubric and clarified when needed.
  • We created a District Phase 1 Summary that reflects the reality of schools, not only district goals.
  • We aligned Phase 1 findings to the Six Pillars, the Continuum, and the DDIER cycle.
  • We communicated key Phase 1 messages and next steps to the board, staff, and community.
  • We stored Phase 1 artifacts and summaries in a shared, easy to access location.
Micro Checklists by Stakeholder
Perspective based
Student quick check
  • I have been asked about my experience in at least one survey, focus group, or conversation.
  • I know that adults are using student input to improve learning and assessment.
Teacher quick check
  • I know where our school is in the Phase 1 process and what comes next.
  • I have contributed at least one piece of evidence or one idea that will show up in our school summary.
Parent or guardian quick check
  • I have had at least one chance to share my experience this year.
  • I can name at least one way the school has acted on family feedback.
Community member quick check
  • I have been invited to share insight about what readiness looks like after graduation.
Board member quick check
  • I can connect Phase 1 findings to at least one board action or discussion.
Staff member quick check
  • I shared at least one observation about student behavior, climate, or support that leaders listened to.
Leadership Huddle Script
A turnkey panel you can drop into principal meetings, Wednesday Leadership Connection, or PLC agendas, with prompts for different stakeholders.
Phase 1 Leadership Huddle
Use in 15 minute check ins
Three big questions for principals to ask their teams
  1. Where is the learner experience already strong in our school. What evidence can we point to that shows students are engaged, challenged, and supported.
  2. Where does our current system get in the way of meaningful learning. Which policies, grading practices, or routines feel misaligned with what we say we value.
  3. If we could only change one thing based on Phase 1 findings. What would have the biggest impact on student learning and sense of readiness this year.
Huddle prompts for teachers
  • If you could redesign one assessment practice at our school, what would it be and why.
  • Which recent learning experience felt most meaningful to students. What made it different.
Huddle prompts for student voice groups
  • When did you feel most proud of your learning this year. What was happening.
  • What makes it easier or harder for you to show what you really know.
Huddle prompts for family sessions
  • When does school feel most connected to your child future.
  • What is one thing you wish we would change about how we communicate learning and progress.
Three watch outs during Phase 1
  • Watch out for blaming people instead of systems. Focus on patterns and structures, not individual teachers or students.
  • Watch out for collecting data you will not use. Only gather evidence that will help you make decisions in Phase 2.
  • Watch out for trying to fix everything at once. Phase 1 is about clarity and priorities, not solving the entire system in one step.
Leadership move
Use this panel as the final slide in your leadership meeting. Ask principals to circle one question and one watch out that they will carry back to their schools this week.
