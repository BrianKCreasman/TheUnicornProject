Phase 5 Overview

Phase 5: Sustainability, Scaling, and Innovation focuses on making the local accountability system durable, adaptable, and integrated into the long term life of the district. The central question becomes, How do we protect what we have built, scale what works, and keep innovating without losing coherence.

On the Local Accountability Continuum, Phase 5 corresponds to Sustain and Evolve the Local Accountability System. In the DDIER cycle, it aligns most closely with Revision and the initiation of the next cycle of Design and Development as learning from Phase 4 fuels fresh improvement.

Phase 5 is grounded in Pillar 2: Governance, Leadership, and Systematic Support, Pillar 5: Evaluating Progress, Impact, and Reporting, and Pillar 6: Sustainability, Partnerships, and Innovation. These pillars help districts move from a project mindset to a system identity.

Why this matters: Without an intentional sustainability and scaling phase, even strong local accountability systems can fade when leaders change or pressures rise. Phase 5 keeps the work alive across time, people, and conditions.

Current On the Ground Conditions and Self Assessment of Context

Before designing sustainability and scaling strategies, districts need a clear sense of their current capacity and context. Phase 5 starts with an honest read of where the system stands in everyday practice.

Profiles of Sustainability Readiness

  • Fragile Innovation: Local accountability practices depend heavily on a few leaders or early adopters.
  • Emerging Stability: Key components are used across schools, but depth and consistency vary.
  • Embedded Practice: Local accountability structures and routines are part of the district’s identity and daily work.

Context Questions for Phase 5

  • Where is the local accountability system most deeply embedded in culture and practice.
  • Where does the system still feel optional, fragile, or misunderstood.
  • How stable are leadership roles, staffing patterns, and funding streams that support the work.
  • What external pressures, such as policy shifts or accountability changes, might affect sustainability.
Why this matters: Sustainability strategies are most effective when they fit the district’s real conditions, not an idealized version of readiness.

Guiding Principles for Sustainability and Scaling

Guiding principles ensure that scaling and innovation reinforce the core vision rather than dilute it. They serve as guardrails when districts adapt the system over time.

Principles for Sustainability

  • Protect the integrity of the core vision for Growth, Mastery, and Readiness while staying open to refinement.
  • Build structures that outlast individual leaders and rely on teams, not single champions.
  • Align policies, budgeting, and staffing with local accountability priorities.
  • Maintain a balanced approach that honors both Vibrant Learning and transparency in reporting.

Principles for Scaling and Innovation

  • Scale practices that have demonstrated positive impact and clear feasibility.
  • Use thoughtful piloting and iteration when introducing new components or extending to new contexts.
  • Ensure that innovation remains coherent with the Portrait of a Learner and Measures of Quality.
  • Engage students, staff, and community partners in co designing future phases of the work.
Why this matters: Clear principles keep Phase 5 focused on deepening and extending what matters most, rather than chasing every new idea.

Preconditions and Readiness Indicators for Phase 5

Certain conditions make Phase 5 more productive. These preconditions signal that the district is ready to move from establishing the system to ensuring its long term life.

Minimum Criteria for Productive Entry

  • Phases 3 and 4 have been completed for at least one full cycle, producing evidence of impact and areas for refinement.
  • Core tools such as NxGRAs, BPI websites, Measures of Quality, and dashboards are implemented across multiple schools.
  • Leadership, staff, and community partners understand the basics of the local accountability system.
  • There is a shared commitment to continuing the work beyond short term initiatives or grants.

Readiness Indicators to Check

  • Evidence that local accountability is referenced in key district documents, such as strategic plans or board policies.
  • Structures for ongoing professional learning and coaching tied to local accountability practices.
  • Operational routines that rely on local evidence for decision making at classroom, school, and district levels.
  • Emerging partnership networks that align with and reinforce the local accountability vision.
Why this matters: These indicators help districts avoid trying to “scale” a system that has not yet reached sufficient maturity.

Designing the Sustainability Architecture

Sustainability is not only about funding. It is about the architecture that makes local accountability simply how the district does its work.

Governance and Policy

  • Board policies that recognize local accountability as part of the district’s accountability approach.
  • Formal roles and responsibilities for local accountability embedded in job descriptions.
  • Advisory councils with clear charters and ongoing responsibilities.
  • Alignment between local accountability and district strategic plans.

People and Capacity

  • Leadership pipelines that prepare future leaders to carry the work forward.
  • Ongoing professional learning structures built around local accountability tools and practices.
  • Teacher leadership roles focused on performance assessment, portfolios, and Measures of Quality.
  • Coaching and support systems that help new staff enter the work smoothly.

Resources and Infrastructure

  • Dedicated budget lines or resource plans for key components like NxGRAs and BPI platforms.
  • Technology infrastructure that supports data collection, dashboards, and artifact curation.
  • Time structures, such as PLCs and leadership meetings, that center local evidence.
  • Flexible resource strategies that allow the district to adapt as needs evolve.

Partnerships and Networks

  • Relationships with universities, research centers, and organizations that support local accountability.
  • Regional or national networks for sharing models, tools, and lessons learned.
  • Community partnerships that provide authentic contexts for performance assessments.
  • Collaborative agreements that reinforce the long term nature of the work.
Sustainability anchor: A strong Phase 5 architecture makes it difficult for the system to disappear quietly. It is woven into people, policy, practice, and partnership.

Core Scaling and Sustainability Workstreams

Workstreams in Phase 5 help districts focus scaling efforts where they will matter most. The goal is to deepen quality while expanding reach.

Workstream 1: Deepening Quality at Scale

  • Identify core practices, such as NxGRAs and BPI curation, that should be consistently implemented across all schools.
  • Develop detailed guides, exemplars, and coaching supports to maintain quality as the work expands.
  • Use calibration processes to ensure consistency in scoring and expectations.
  • Monitor implementation patterns and provide targeted support where quality lags.

Workstream 2: Extending Reach and Access

  • Expand local accountability practices to additional grade bands, programs, or student groups.
  • Ensure students in specialized programs, such as alternative education or career pathways, are fully included.
  • Address barriers that keep some students from accessing vibrant learning experiences.
  • Use Findings from Phase 4 to guide where expansion should be prioritized.

Workstream 3: Innovation and Continuous Improvement

  • Identify areas where the system still needs new tools or approaches to fully honor the Portrait of a Learner.
  • Launch focused pilots that build on what has been learned in earlier phases.
  • Use the DDIER cycle explicitly to guide iterative innovation within a stable system.
  • Invite student and community co designers into new rounds of assessment and accountability design.

Workstream 4: Communication, Storytelling, and Advocacy

  • Develop ongoing communication campaigns that keep local accountability visible and understood.
  • Highlight student stories and artifacts that show the value of the system.
  • Prepare materials that help staff and community members explain the system to new audiences.
  • Use these stories to advocate for supportive policies at district, regional, and state levels.
Practical tip: These workstreams can be sequenced over multiple years, allowing districts to move from establishing, to stabilizing, to growing the system without losing focus.

Progress and Impact Indicators in Phase 5

Phase 5 focuses on whether local accountability is becoming a durable part of the system and whether its impact is reaching more students and communities.

Progress Indicators for Sustainability

  • Local accountability referenced routinely in board meetings, leadership sessions, and school improvement plans.
  • Stable or growing investment of time and resources dedicated to core components.
  • New leaders and staff are oriented to the system as part of standard induction.
  • Structures such as advisory councils and PLCs continue regardless of personnel changes.

Impact Indicators for Scaling and Innovation

  • Increasing numbers of students engaging in performance assessments and BPI curation.
  • Broader representation of student groups in high quality vibrant learning experiences.
  • Clear examples of how innovations from pilots have improved the overall system.
  • Recognition from external partners or networks for the district’s local accountability work.
Why this matters: These indicators help districts see whether the work is deepening, widening, and evolving rather than plateauing or shrinking.

Evidence and Key Questions for Sustainability and Scaling

Phase 5 uses evidence not only to understand impact, but to understand the health of the system over time.

Evidence to Monitor

  • Implementation data showing participation in NxGRAs, BPI curation, and Measures of Quality across years.
  • Longitudinal patterns in student outcomes, engagement, and postsecondary readiness.
  • Trends in staff retention, especially among those deeply engaged in local accountability work.
  • Shifts in community perception and trust as reflected in surveys and public feedback.
  • Patterns in resource allocation aligned to local accountability priorities.

Key Questions

  • Is local accountability still aligned to our evolving understanding of what students need to thrive.
  • Where is the system strongest and most resilient, and why.
  • Where is the system fragile or overly dependent on specific people or conditions.
  • How are new innovations affecting coherence, equity, and clarity.
  • What adjustments are needed to keep the system both stable and responsive in the next cycle.

Learning Loop: Reflection and Next Cycle Design

  • What has Phase 5 revealed about which components of the system are core and which are flexible.
  • What new questions have emerged that should shape the next round of design and development.
  • How will leadership transitions and changing conditions be anticipated and planned for in the next cycle.
  • What commitments do we need to renew explicitly with staff, students, families, and partners.
  • How will we communicate the story of this cycle and the vision for the next one in clear, hopeful, and honest ways.
Use in practice: These questions can structure an annual “Local Accountability Summit” where the district closes one cycle and opens the next.

Stakeholder Roles in Phase 5 Sustainability and Scaling

Long term sustainability depends on broad ownership. Phase 5 clarifies how each stakeholder group keeps the work moving and evolving.

Superintendent and Cabinet

  • Ensure local accountability is reflected in district strategy, budgeting, and staffing decisions.
  • Model commitment to the work across leadership transitions and political cycles.
  • Develop succession plans that prepare future leaders to steward the system.
  • Build external partnerships that reinforce the vision and provide additional support.

Board of Education

  • Adopt policies that recognize and support local accountability as part of district accountability.
  • Ask consistent questions about how local evidence informs decisions.
  • Support investments that protect core components and capacity building.
  • Champion the work publicly and with state and regional partners.

District and School Leaders

  • Integrate local accountability practices into daily leadership routines.
  • Develop leadership teams that share responsibility for sustaining the work.
  • Support new leaders and staff in understanding and enacting local accountability.
  • Use evidence from Phase 5 to adjust schedules, supports, and expectations.

Teachers and Staff

  • Continue to refine and use local accountability tools as part of high quality instruction.
  • Serve as mentors and guides for colleagues new to the system.
  • Participate in improvement cycles that shape new iterations of tasks, rubrics, and routines.
  • Share classroom stories that demonstrate the long term value of the work.

Students, Families, and Community Partners

  • Hold the district accountable for maintaining meaningful, relevant definitions of success.
  • Continue to contribute voice and partnership to new cycles of design and evaluation.
  • Celebrate and share examples of student growth, mastery, and readiness emerging from the system.
  • Support efforts to advocate for local accountability at regional and state levels.

Best Practices Across Stakeholders

  • Keep the language of local accountability visible and consistent across years.
  • Build routines that new people can step into without starting over.
  • Revisit and renew agreements about the vision and core components on a regular cadence.
  • Recognize and celebrate the people and teams who sustain the work year after year.
Why this matters: When stakeholders see themselves as stewards of the system, sustainability becomes a shared responsibility, not a central office task.

Risks and Mitigation Strategies in Phase 5

Sustainability work carries real risks. Anticipating and planning for them helps maintain momentum.

Risk: Leadership Turnover

  • Changes in leadership lead to loss of focus or abandonment of local accountability.
  • Mitigation: Embed the work in policy, structures, and culture so it is not dependent on individuals.
  • Mitigation: Build onboarding processes that orient new leaders to the system and its history.

Risk: Initiative Fatigue

  • Staff perceive local accountability as one more initiative rather than how the district does its work.
  • Mitigation: Streamline competing initiatives and show how local accountability integrates and focuses the work.
  • Mitigation: Highlight ways the system reduces redundancy and clarifies priorities.

Risk: Drift from Core Vision

  • Over time, pressures or new ideas pull the system away from the original purpose.
  • Mitigation: Revisit and reaffirm the Portrait of a Learner and core principles annually.
  • Mitigation: Use the DDIER cycle to introduce innovation within a coherent frame.

Risk: Overreliance on External Support

  • The system depends too heavily on outside organizations or grant funding.
  • Mitigation: Build internal capacity and gradually shift key functions to district teams.
  • Mitigation: Use partnerships strategically while planning for long term self sufficiency.
Why this matters: Proactive mitigation helps the district stay committed, coherent, and resilient through change.

Phase 5: Key Outcome

By the end of Phase 5, the district should have a stable, evolving local accountability system that is embedded in culture, structures, and partnerships and ready for the next intentional cycle of design, development, implementation, evaluation, and revision.

System Level Outcome

  • Local accountability is widely recognized as part of the district’s identity and daily practice.
  • Core components such as NxGRAs, BPI websites, and Measures of Quality are implemented with consistency and quality.
  • Leadership, staff, and community members understand their roles in sustaining the work.
  • Partnerships and networks reinforce rather than compete with the district’s vision.

Readiness for the Next Cycle

  • A clear plan for the next DDIER cycle, informed by Phase 4 findings and Phase 5 learning.
  • Identified priorities for future design and development, such as new assessment types or reporting innovations.
  • Structures in place to bring new staff and leaders into the work without starting from scratch.
  • A shared understanding that local accountability is an ongoing journey, not a finished project.
Why this matters: When Phase 5 concludes with a living system and a clear next cycle, local accountability becomes a durable engine for continuous improvement and vibrant learning.

Practice in Action: Phase 5 in Fleming County Schools

In Fleming County Schools, Phase 5 means ensuring that Vibrant Accountability is not a moment in time, but the way the district does its work, year after year. Sustainability, scaling, and innovation are treated as part of the same journey.

Embedding Vibrant Accountability in Daily Practice

  • Local accountability is integrated into strategic planning, leadership meetings, and school improvement routines.
  • Leadership transitions include intentional orientation to the Portrait of a Learner, BPI websites, NxGRAs, and Measures of Quality.
  • Teacher leaders and principals serve as ongoing champions and designers of the work.
  • Students and families recognize the language of Growth, Mastery, and Readiness as part of how success is defined.

Growing and Innovating the System

  • New NxGRAs and BRIDGE Performance Assessments are developed and refined each year based on student interests and emerging needs.
  • Partnerships with organizations and networks help bring fresh ideas and support into the work.
  • Lessons learned from implementation and evaluation cycles inform each new round of design.
  • Vibrant Accountability continues to evolve while staying grounded in the district’s values and community context.
Field insight: Treating Phase 5 as an ongoing stance, not a one time step, helps Fleming County keep the work moving forward regardless of change.

Implementation Supports and Tools for Phase 5

Sustainability and scaling require practical supports that make it easier to keep the work going and growing.

Core Sustainability Tools

  • Role specific onboarding guides that explain the local accountability system to new leaders, teachers, and staff.
  • Annual cycle maps that show when design, implementation, evaluation, and revision activities occur.
  • Templates for updating strategic plans, improvement plans, and board reports with local evidence.
  • Toolkits such as “See Toolkit: Sustaining Local Accountability Year Over Year.”

Supports for Scaling and Innovation

  • Design labs or innovation teams focused on new NxGRAs, reporting formats, or partnership models.
  • Networks or collaboratives that provide thought partnership and shared resources.
  • Professional learning modules that prepare staff to lead new components of the system.
  • References to resources such as “See Template: Multi Year Scaling Plan” and “See Guide: Partnering for Local Accountability Innovation.”
Why this matters: Tools and supports make it possible for busy educators to sustain and grow a complex system without losing sight of students and learning.
