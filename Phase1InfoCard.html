Phase 1: Laying the Groundwork

Understanding Current Conditions and Readiness
Purpose: Phase 1 is the truth-telling phase. It prevents the most common failure pattern in accountability redesign: building tools before there is shared clarity on purpose, evidence, and definitions of success.
▶
Clear Definition and Description
Why this phase matters

Phase 1 requires disciplined clarity, not speed. Before a district designs new assessments, dashboards, or reporting routines, it must confront three foundational realities:

  • How success is defined locally
  • What the current system is actually producing
  • How ready the community is to shift from a test-centered narrative to a learning-centered one

In this phase, the district conducts a readiness scan across learning experiences, existing measures, evidence quality, stakeholder trust, and implementation capacity. The work highlights misalignments between vision and practice, identifies which forms of evidence are credible and actionable, and clarifies where trust and understanding need to be strengthened.

The outcome is not consensus for its own sake. The outcome is informed decisions and clear guardrails that will shape every design choice in the phases that follow.

▶
Indicators of Readiness
Signals you can see
  • The district has a clear, concise, stakeholder-friendly, and locally defined description of student success that is understood and consistently referenced across stakeholder groups.
  • The district has an honest, evidence-informed picture of what students are actually experiencing day to day.
  • The district understands what its current measures do well, what they distort, and what they fail to capture.
  • The district has begun distinguishing between compliance artifacts and meaningful evidence of learning.
  • Educators share a baseline understanding of assessment purposes and how different assessments serve different roles.
  • Stakeholders believe that accountability will be used to improve learning, not punish individuals.
  • Leaders demonstrate the ability to hold purpose, resist premature solutions, and protect the integrity of the process.
  • The district understands the current accountability narrative and how ready the community is to evolve it.

Reflective Questions
Diagnostic and decision-guiding

Purpose and Definition of Success

  • Can students, educators, board members, and families explain what “success” means here without state jargon?
  • Is our definition of success observable in classrooms and student work?

Evidence and Measures

  • Which current measures help us understand learning, and which mainly serve compliance?
  • Where do we confuse activity, completion, or scores with evidence of learning?

Student Experience

  • What would a student say learning feels like here on an average day?
  • How often do students have opportunities to demonstrate learning in authentic ways?

Trust and Narrative

  • Do stakeholders believe accountability exists to support improvement or to assign blame?
  • Where does skepticism come from, and what evidence would help rebuild trust?

Leadership Readiness

  • Are leaders willing to pause design work until clarity is achieved?
  • What pressures are pushing us toward solutions before we are ready?
Anchor question: If an external reviewer asked, “What does success mean here, and how do you know?”, could the district answer clearly without pointing to a test score?
▶
Why This Phase Cannot Be Rushed
Risk reduction

Phase 1 is where credibility is earned. It is where the district proves it is serious about learning, not just reporting. Skipping or compressing this phase does not save time. It creates fragility. When Phase 1 is done well, later phases move faster, encounter less resistance, and produce evidence the community trusts.

If Phase 1 feels uncomfortable, it is probably working.
