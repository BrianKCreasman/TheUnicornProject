Phase 2 on a Page. Designing the Architecture
Framework. Evidence and Learning Loop Framework. Reporting and Transparency Framework. Phase 2 Key Outcome
Goal. Turn Phase 1 findings into a clear, usable local accountability architecture that defines what counts as evidence, how it gets validated, and how it gets reported in a way the community can understand.
In plain language
  • We define Growth, Mastery, and Readiness in ways that are observable and provable.
  • We decide what counts as evidence, what quality looks like, and how we keep evidence credible.
  • We design how schools will collect, review, and share evidence without creating a paperwork system.
  • We build reporting that is understandable to families and defensible to professionals.
By the end of Phase 2, we have:
  • A Phase 2 Design Package that describes the system architecture.
  • A clear evidence taxonomy with quality criteria and examples.
  • Draft reporting templates and plain language interpretation guidance.
  • Decision rules and governance routines for continuous improvement.
  • A pilot-ready plan aligned to the architecture (Phase 3 dependency).
Who needs to understand this
  • Board members and district leaders who will protect the integrity of the model.
  • School leaders and teacher leaders who will operationalize evidence routines.
  • Students, families, and community partners who need clarity and trust in reporting.
Most people only need this view. The detailed steps, templates, and integrity checks are for the teams actually building Phase 2 outputs.

The Three Big Moves in Phase 2
Instead of many disconnected design conversations, Phase 2 can be remembered as three big moves.
Big Move 1
Define the System Claims and Evidence
  • Finalize Growth, Mastery, Readiness definitions.
  • Write the claim-evidence map for each outcome.
  • Identify what counts as evidence and what does not.
  • Stress test definitions with real examples.
Output. A coherent definition layer that can be taught, used, and defended.
Big Move 2
Build Quality Criteria and Calibration Routines
  • Define evidence quality criteria and minimum expectations.
  • Draft rubrics and exemplars that show levels of quality.
  • Create calibration routines for scoring integrity.
  • Establish storage and naming conventions for artifacts.
Output. A defensible evidence system that is fair and consistent.
Big Move 3
Design Reporting and Governance That Hold
  • Design reporting layers and public-facing interpretation guides.
  • Decide what is public, what is internal, and why.
  • Set decision rules and governance routines to prevent drift.
  • Produce a pilot-ready implementation plan for Phase 3.
Output. Reporting that is understandable to the public and useful for improvement.

1. Orientation to Phase 2
Why Phase 2 exists, what success looks like, and how leaders can use this guide as a build plan.
Purpose of Phase 2
Build the architecture
The purpose of Phase 2 is to turn Phase 1 findings into a coherent, usable design for a local accountability model.
  • Define Growth, Mastery, and Readiness with observable evidence anchors.
  • Create a claim-evidence map that ties outcomes to defensible sources.
  • Define evidence quality standards and calibration routines.
  • Design reporting that is interpretable to non-educators and useful for improvement.
  • Set governance and decision rules to prevent drift.
  • Produce a pilot-ready plan and tools for Phase 3.
A simple test
If your design can be explained, taught, and defended in plain language, you are building the right kind of system.

  • Principals and teacher leaders who test feasibility and calibrate evidence expectations.
  • LAAC members who stress test legitimacy and interpretability.
  • Board members who will protect integrity and communicate clearly with the public.

3. Roles, Responsibilities, and the Local Accountability Advisory Council
Clear lanes for Phase 2 design so the system stays coherent and the community stays included.
System Level Roles
District and board
Superintendent and district leaders
  • Protect coherence. Limit scope. Keep decisions disciplined.
  • Own the decision log and publish ‚Äúwhat we decided and why‚Äù summaries.
  • Ensure evidence quality and calibration routines are realistic.
  • Coordinate LAAC testing sessions and share back outcomes.
  • Prepare board learning moments focused on interpretability and integrity.
Board members
  • Learn the system claims and what evidence supports each claim.
  • Ask questions about equity, credibility, and public understanding.
  • Protect policy and resources that make the system implementable.
  • Support clear public messaging that avoids scoreboards and rankings.
School and Classroom Roles
School and classroom level
Principals and leadership teams
  • Test feasibility. Translate design into school routines.
  • Lead staff calibration sessions using real student work.
  • Identify implementation barriers early and log them.
  • Ensure equity of opportunity so evidence is not distorted.
Teachers and staff
  • Test evidence criteria against real tasks and student work.
  • Identify which routines fit PLCs and which need redesign.
  • Help build exemplars and non-exemplars for clarity.
Students and families
  • Stress test whether evidence rules match lived experience.
  • Verify that reporting templates are understandable and fair.
  • Help identify where communication will break down.
Local Accountability Advisory Council (LAAC) in Phase 2
Co-design and legitimacy
Purpose
The LAAC ensures Phase 2 design is locally owned, understandable, and aligned to community values.
  • Review and stress test definitions, evidence rules, and reporting prototypes.
  • Surface interpretability issues before public reporting begins.
  • Flag equity risks and access-to-opportunity gaps that impact evidence.
  • Recommend revisions and help communicate design decisions back to the community.
Expectations in Phase 2
  • The LAAC meets at least monthly during Phase 2 design work.
  • Each meeting reviews a tangible draft (definitions, rubric, reporting mockup).
  • District publishes ‚Äúwhat we decided and why‚Äù within two weeks.
  • LAAC members test interpretability with peers and report back themes.

Leadership Huddle. 20 Minutes to Keep Phase 2 on Track
A repeatable huddle for design sprints. Tight, clear, and grounded in artifacts.
Huddle Agenda
20 minutes
1) What decision are we making this week
  • Name the design decision in one sentence.
  • Confirm who decides and what input is needed.
2) What artifact are we using to decide
  • One draft definition, rubric row, or reporting mockup.
  • One example and one non-example.
3) What did stakeholders tell us
  • One clarity signal and one confusion signal.
  • What we will share back, and when.
4) What is the next build action
  • Assign an owner and a due date.
  • Log the decision and rationale.
Non-negotiable huddle output
A one paragraph decision log entry with the artifact link, rationale, and next action.
Huddle Prompts
Use as-is
  • What claim are we making, and what evidence proves it.
  • Where could this design create inequity of opportunity.
  • What part of this would a parent misunderstand and why.
  • What is the minimum viable version that still protects integrity.
  • What will we stop doing because of this design.
Watch for this drift
If huddles turn into vague updates without artifacts, stop. Phase 2 is a build cycle, not a committee cycle.
