Phase 2 Overview

In Phase 1, the district defined success for students and built a shared logic model for local accountability. Phase 2: Development is where that design turns into real systems, tools, and routines that can live in classrooms, schools, and boardrooms. The central question shifts from what success looks like to How will we consistently see, support, and document that success across classrooms and schools.

Phase 2 is anchored in Pillar 2: Governance, Leadership, and Systematic Support, Pillar 3: Community Engagement, Ownership, and Advocacy, and Pillar 4: Data Validation, Analysis, and Utilization. Together, these pillars ensure that the emerging accountability system is not a loose collection of projects, but a coherent architecture that can be led, supported, and trusted over time.

On the Local Accountability Continuum, Phase 2 corresponds to Build the Local Accountability System. This is where districts design and prototype performance assessments, surveys, dashboards, Measures of Quality, and evidence routines that align directly to the Portrait of a Learner and the logic model established in Phase 1.

Why this matters: Development is not about building everything at once. It is about building the few systems that matter most, testing them in the real world, and aligning them tightly to the purpose and portrait defined in Phase 1.

Current On the Ground Conditions and Self Assessment of Context

Before moving deep into Phase 2, districts identify their present state by selecting from realistic, K-12 specific profiles. These profiles capture common combinations of instructional practice, assessment culture, data use, and community trust. The goal is to name what is true now, not what leaders hope will be true later.

Self Assessment of Context

  • Select one or more current state profiles that best match classroom practice, assessment use, and culture.
  • Document key strengths, tensions, and non negotiables that shape how new tools will be received.
  • Identify variation across schools and grade levels so Phase 2 work does not assume a single starting point.
  • Capture a short narrative that describes the system as it truly functions today, in student and teacher terms.

Using Context to Shape Phase 2

  • Align pilot sites and workstreams to current strengths so early wins are visible and credible.
  • Surface where legacy practices or policies will complicate the use of new evidence tools.
  • Clarify realistic pacing, support, and communication needs based on the actual conditions in schools.
  • Share the context narrative with advisory groups so community partners understand the starting point.
Why this matters: When Phase 2 starts from realism instead of aspiration, design choices are grounded, pilots are better targeted, and staff are less likely to experience the work as disconnected from their daily reality.

Guiding Principles for Phase 2 Development

Guiding principles keep Phase 2 anchored to deeper beliefs about learning, accountability, and community. They shape decision making when tradeoffs arise and help teams avoid recreating old systems with new language.

Principles for Learning and Evidence

  • Evidence should illuminate student learning, not simply sort or label students and schools.
  • Tools must connect directly to the Portrait of a Learner and priority standards, not sit alongside them.
  • Local measures should elevate student voice, agency, and real world performance, not just compliance tasks.
  • Data must be interpreted in context, with an emphasis on growth, improvement, and readiness.

Principles for System Design

  • Build for usability first, then sophistication, so tools can be used well by real people in real time.
  • Integrate new tools into existing routines where possible instead of layering on more work.
  • Design with the end user, not just for them, by involving teachers, students, families, and partners.
  • Prioritize coherence across levels so classroom evidence flows naturally into school and district decisions.
Why this matters: Clear principles prevent Phase 2 from drifting back to test only thinking and help leaders make choices that are consistent with the district vision and local values.

Preconditions and Readiness Indicators for Phase Entry

Not every district is ready to engage fully in Phase 2. Minimum readiness criteria protect staff from premature implementation and create stable conditions for meaningful development work.

Minimum Criteria for Productive Entry

  • A clearly articulated Portrait of a Learner and district purpose statement for local accountability.
  • An agreed upon logic model that links learning experiences, evidence, and outcomes across the system.
  • A core design team with representation from central office, school leadership, teachers, and community partners.
  • Baseline understanding among leaders that Phase 2 is about system design, not immediate high stakes use.

Readiness Indicators to Check

  • Leaders can describe Phase 1 decisions and products without relying on slides or scripts.
  • Schools have basic collaborative structures where pilots and feedback cycles can realistically occur.
  • There is at least a minimal trust foundation with staff and community to support honest pilot feedback.
  • Time and support have been intentionally carved out in calendars and professional learning plans.
Why this matters: Entering Phase 2 without these preconditions increases the likelihood that tools will be built but not used, or that staff will experience local accountability as one more initiative rather than a coherent shift.

How Development, Pillars 2–4, and the Continuum Connect

Development (DDIER)

The Development phase translates the logic model into concrete structures. District teams design and draft performance assessments, student portfolio expectations, survey instruments, Measures of Quality rubrics, and early dashboard prototypes. They also map the supports, timelines, and guardrails needed so these tools can be used consistently, not just once.

Pillars 2–4 in Phase 2

Pillar 2 ensures leadership structures, roles, and decision making processes are clear so tools do not float without ownership. Pillar 3 keeps families, students, and community partners involved as co designers of surveys, reporting tools, and measures, which builds trust and relevance. Pillar 4 guides how data will be validated, analyzed, and used so that evidence is credible and improvement focused, not punitive.

Build the Local Accountability System

In the Continuum, this phase is where the district builds the core of its local accountability system. Performance assessments, Measures of Quality, student portfolios, and stakeholder surveys are designed to make Growth, Mastery, and Readiness visible. These tools are built to function together as a system, rather than as isolated initiatives.

From Logic Model to System Blueprint

A strong Phase 2 begins with a clear system blueprint. The blueprint shows how classroom assessments, school level measures, district level indicators, and public reporting all connect back to the Portrait of a Learner and the logic model. It also clarifies who owns each piece, how often it will be used, and how evidence will move across levels.

Classroom Level

  • Next Generation Readiness Assessments aligned to priority standards and Portrait traits.
  • Student digital portfolios and BPI Websites that capture compelling evidence of learning.
  • Formative checks, reflection prompts, and student surveys that capture agency and engagement.
  • Teacher feedback routines tied to standards and durable skills.

School Level

  • Measures of Quality indicators for Vibrant Learning, Vibrant Outcomes, and Vibrant Culture.
  • Common performance tasks or BRIDGE Performance Assessments in key grades and subjects.
  • Staff climate and culture surveys tied to Vibrant Ecosystem conditions.
  • School improvement plans that reference local accountability evidence, not just state metrics.

District Level

  • District dashboards that combine Growth, Mastery, and Readiness indicators with culture and community measures.
  • Quarterly Measures of Quality reports that tell a balanced story of student learning and conditions.
  • Local Accountability Advisory Council reviews that examine data through community priorities.
  • Professional learning plans aligned to gaps identified in local evidence.

Public Reporting

  • Family facing reports that explain Growth, Mastery, and Readiness in plain language.
  • Community dashboards that highlight student work, local projects, and durable skills in action.
  • Board reports that connect measures to decisions about resources, policies, and supports.
  • Annual summaries that show progress on the Portrait of a Learner and Measures of Quality.

Core Development Workstreams

To keep Phase 2 manageable, districts often organize the work into a small number of focused workstreams. Each workstream has a clear purpose, a small design team, timelines, and quality criteria. The grid below offers an example of how Phase 2 work can be organized.

Workstream 1: Performance Assessment and Portfolios

  • Design and refine Next Generation Readiness Assessments in priority content areas and grades.
  • Clarify expectations for student digital portfolios and BPI Websites, including curation cycles and reflection prompts.
  • Develop rubrics and proficiency scales that blend standards, Portrait traits, and durable skills.
  • Pilot tasks in classrooms and gather feedback from students and teachers on clarity, relevance, and workload.

Workstream 2: Measures of Quality and School Indicators

  • Refine or develop Measures of Quality indicators for Vibrant Learning, Outcomes, Culture, Community, and Ecosystem.
  • Define scoring ranges, evidence sources, and documentation expectations for each indicator.
  • Align school improvement planning templates to Measures of Quality so schools can act on indicator results.
  • Prototype a scoring and reporting process that schools can complete in a reasonable, predictable rhythm.

Workstream 3: Surveys, Voice, and Culture

  • Design or refine staff, student, and family surveys aligned to Vibrant Accountability and the Portrait of a Learner.
  • Decide how often surveys will be given, how results will be shared, and how feedback will be acted on.
  • Ensure survey items are clear, accessible, and available in needed languages.
  • Develop simple visualizations that highlight strengths, concerns, and trends over time.

Workstream 4: Dashboards, Reporting, and Storytelling

  • Prototype internal dashboards for leaders that combine Growth, Mastery, Readiness, and culture indicators.
  • Design family and community facing views that emphasize clarity, balance, and plain language.
  • Align reporting cycles with Measures of Quality and board reporting schedules to reduce duplication.
  • Create templates that highlight student examples and artifacts alongside quantitative data.
Practical tip: Districts do not need separate teams for every workstream. The same core coalition can rotate focus, as long as the work is sequenced, scoped, and supported with realistic timelines.

Milestones and Progress Indicators for Phase 2

Milestones for Phase 2 are not summative test scores. They are practical signals that the work is moving in the right direction and that tools are becoming usable, coherent, and trusted.

In the Workstreams

  • A first round of performance assessments and portfolio expectations has been piloted in a defined set of classrooms.
  • Draft Measures of Quality rubrics have been tested by at least one school in each level or configuration.
  • Survey prototypes for at least two stakeholder groups have been administered and debriefed.
  • At least one dashboard or reporting prototype has been reviewed by leaders and community members.

In the Culture and Conversations

  • Teachers and leaders reference Portrait traits and local measures when they talk about student learning.
  • Pilot feedback sessions generate concrete revisions rather than generic approval or resistance.
  • Local accountability evidence shows up in school improvement plans and leadership meetings.
  • Advisory groups and board members can describe how local measures add insight beyond state tests.
Why this matters: These milestones help districts ask a simple question throughout Phase 2, are we on track to build a usable, trusted system, or are we only generating more documents and meetings.

Evidence and Key Questions in Development

Evidence for Phase 2 focuses on whether the tools being built are usable, coherent, and aligned to the portrait and logic model. Districts monitor both the quality of the products and the experience of the people who are piloting them.

Evidence of Efficacy

  • Pilot teachers report that Next Generation Readiness Assessments are understandable, aligned to standards, and worth the instructional time they require.
  • Student work and BPI Websites from pilot classrooms show visible connections to Portrait traits and durable skills, not just content coverage.
  • Measures of Quality rubrics and evidence guides are clear enough that different schools can apply them with consistent interpretations.
  • Survey pilots yield response patterns that make sense, with items that distinguish between trust, culture, workload, and leadership.
  • Early dashboard views are readable, accurate, and connected to decisions that leaders actually make.

Key Questions for Sensemaking

  • Where do teachers, students, and families find the new tools helpful, and where do they feel confusing, duplicative, or disconnected from daily work.
  • Do the tools we are building make Growth, Mastery, and Readiness more visible, or are they adding more noise to an already crowded system.
  • How are we validating the quality and reliability of our measures, including inter rater consistency for rubrics and clarity in survey items.
  • What feedback from pilot sites or advisory groups has led us to adjust or simplify tools, and what did we learn from those changes.
  • Are we building enough space for professional learning and collaboration so that staff feel supported, not overwhelmed.

Learning Loop: Reflection and Next Steps

  • What surprised us during this phase, either about our tools or about how our system actually works in practice.
  • Where did we see the most meaningful improvement in clarity, coherence, or student evidence, and what made that possible.
  • Which tools or routines created unnecessary friction, and how will we simplify or retire them before scaling further.
  • What adjustments are needed in our supports, timelines, or communication before we move into Phase 3 Implementation.
  • How will we carry forward what we have learned about evidence that truly changes conversations about learning.
Use in practice: These prompts can be built into debrief sessions after each pilot cycle, helping teams refine tools before scaling them and reinforcing that improvement is cyclical, not linear.

Stakeholder Roles in Phase 2 Development

In Phase 2, stakeholder roles shift from defining success to building and testing the tools that will make that success visible. Engagement, empowerment, and reciprocity remain essential. Each group needs a clear role in shaping, piloting, and refining the systems that will later be used to evaluate schools.

School and District Leaders

  • Identify pilot sites and teams for each workstream, ensuring a mix of grade levels, school contexts, and staff voices.
  • Protect time for pilots by adjusting expectations, calendars, or existing initiatives where possible.
  • Model transparency by sharing what is being piloted, what feedback is emerging, and what will change as a result.
  • Clarify which tools are in draft, pilot, or finalized status so staff are not guessing about expectations.

Teachers and School Staff

  • Co design and pilot performance assessments, Measures of Quality evidence routines, and survey processes.
  • Provide concrete feedback on directions, time demands, scoring, and student response.
  • Share student work and examples that illustrate how tools can be refined for clarity and relevance.
  • Help communicate with families and students about what these new tools are and why they matter.

Students

  • Participate in pilots of performance assessments and provide feedback on relevance, challenge, and opportunity for voice and choice.
  • Use BPI Websites and portfolios to curate evidence and tell the story of their learning journey.
  • Respond to student surveys and focus groups about engagement, support, and clarity of expectations.
  • Serve as co designers of reflection prompts, portfolio structures, or presentation formats where appropriate.

Parents and Guardians

  • Participate in feedback cycles on family facing reports, dashboards, and communication tools.
  • Offer input on what information is most helpful for understanding their child’s growth, mastery, and readiness.
  • Respond to pilot surveys and town halls to shape how local accountability information is shared.
  • Partner with schools to support students as they engage in new performance tasks and portfolio work.

Community and Partners

  • Co design or host real world performance tasks, exhibitions, and internships that generate authentic evidence.
  • Review dashboards and reports to ensure community priorities are visible, not hidden in technical details.
  • Serve on advisory groups that examine data, question assumptions, and suggest new indicators or evidence sources.
  • Champion the district’s local accountability work in public settings, highlighting why it matters for the community’s future.

Best Practices Across Stakeholders

  • Use rapid feedback cycles where pilots are short, feedback is captured quickly, and revisions are visible to participants.
  • Balance surveys and focus groups with hands on design sessions where people help build rubrics, dashboards, and reports.
  • Share back "you said, we did" summaries so stakeholders see how their input shaped final tools.
  • Recognize and celebrate pilot sites and teams as early adopters who are helping the district learn, not as test subjects being judged.
Why this matters: When stakeholders are part of building the system, they are more likely to trust and use it. Phase 2 is an ideal time to deepen that trust, long before high stakes reporting begins.

Risks and Mitigation Strategies in Phase 2

Any serious development work in local accountability will surface tensions and risks. Anticipating those risks and planning mitigation strategies builds resilience and helps districts navigate complexity with more confidence.

Risk: Initiative Overload

  • Leaders launch new tools without retiring or consolidating existing assessments and reporting requirements.
  • Mitigation: Map all current assessments and data collections, then intentionally sunset or combine those that no longer serve the vision.
  • Mitigation: Communicate clearly which tools are replacing legacy practices and how workload will be kept reasonable.

Risk: Low Trust or Fear of Judgment

  • Staff or community members worry that pilots will be used to evaluate them rather than improve the system.
  • Mitigation: Frame pilots as learning cycles, not accountability events, and keep early data use low stakes.
  • Mitigation: Share examples of how feedback has led to tool revisions rather than criticism of individuals.

Risk: Fragmentation and Incoherence

  • Different workstreams or departments design tools that do not align or speak to one another.
  • Mitigation: Use a single system blueprint as a touchstone for every design decision in Phase 2.
  • Mitigation: Hold regular cross team integration sessions to check for alignment, duplication, and gaps.

Risk: Underestimating Support Needs

  • Tools are launched without adequate training, coaching, or time for collaborative scoring and reflection.
  • Mitigation: Build professional learning and coaching into the design of each tool, not as an afterthought.
  • Mitigation: Start with smaller pilots and grow gradually, rather than launching everything to everyone at once.
Why this matters: When districts name risks openly and plan mitigation strategies, Phase 2 becomes a space for disciplined learning rather than reactive problem solving.

Phase 2: Key Outcome

By the end of Phase 2, the district should have more than ideas and pilots. It should have a small set of field tested, coherent tools and routines that are ready to scale and refine in Phase 3 Implementation. These outcomes make it possible to move forward without constant reinvention.

Anchor Tools and Measures

  • A bank of Next Generation Readiness Assessments and BRIDGE Performance Assessments in key content areas and grade bands.
  • Clear expectations for BPI Websites and digital portfolios, including artifacts, reflections, and curation timelines.
  • Finalized Measures of Quality rubrics and guidance documents that schools can use with confidence.
  • Pilot tested survey instruments for students, staff, and families, aligned to Vibrant Accountability and culture priorities.

System Processes and Supports

  • Documented workflows for how evidence is collected, scored, reviewed, and reported at classroom, school, and district levels.
  • Professional learning plans that build capacity for performance assessment, portfolio use, and data informed reflection.
  • Calibration routines for scoring student work and Measures of Quality indicators across schools.
  • A schedule of local accountability cycles, including when evidence is gathered, reviewed, and shared publicly.

Governance, Engagement, and Communication

  • A clearly defined role for the Local Accountability Advisory Council in reviewing Phase 2 products and recommending refinements.
  • Board agreements about how local evidence will be used alongside state accountability results.
  • A communications plan that explains new tools to families, staff, and the community in phases, not all at once.
  • Templates for board and community reports that foreground student work, stories, and clear visuals.

Readiness for Phase 3 Implementation

  • Clarity about which tools will move from pilot to scale in the next year, and which will stay in development.
  • Identified early implementation indicators that show whether tools are being used as intended in schools.
  • Agreement on where to start small and where district wide consistency is essential from the beginning.
  • A shared understanding that Phase 3 will focus on practice, feedback, and continuous improvement, not on redesigning tools from scratch.
Why this matters: When these outcomes are in place, Phase 3 Implementation can focus on coaching, support, and continuous improvement instead of constant tool revision.

Practice in Action: Phase 2 in Fleming County Schools

In Fleming County Schools, Phase 2 Development has focused on turning the Portrait of a Learner, BRIDGE Performance Indicators, and Measures of Quality into concrete, usable systems. The goal has been simple and ambitious. Build tools that help students show who they are and what they can do, while giving leaders and the community a clearer view of Growth, Mastery, and Readiness.

Building the Performance Assessment System

  • Teachers have co designed Next Generation Readiness Assessments across grades and subjects, using the Portrait and BRIDGE Indicators as anchors.
  • Rubrics connect standards, durable skills, and real world application so students can see why their work matters.
  • Student BPI Websites serve as the central place where artifacts from these performance tasks are curated and reflected on over time.
  • Pilot cycles have been used to refine task clarity, workload, and scoring before scaling to all classrooms.

Measures of Quality and Local Indicators

  • Measures of Quality 4.1 has been developed as a practical framework that connects Vibrant Learning, Vibrant Outcomes, Vibrant Culture, Vibrant Community, and the Vibrant Ecosystem.
  • Schools gather evidence for each measure using student work, surveys, observations, and local data.
  • Rubrics and exemplars support consistent scoring and interpretation across buildings.
  • Quarterly reporting cycles are being built so families and the community can see progress throughout the year, not only at the end.

Surveys, Voice, and Culture

  • Staff and student surveys are being aligned to Vibrant Accountability and the Measures of Quality, emphasizing trust, clarity, and support.
  • Results are shared back with schools and the community in simple visuals that invite conversation and action.
  • Feedback from survey pilots has led to revisions in item wording, response scales, and reporting formats.
  • Student and family voices are used to refine how culture and climate data are interpreted and acted upon.

Dashboards and Storytelling

  • Prototype dashboards are being designed to display Growth, Mastery, and Readiness alongside culture and community indicators.
  • Leaders are working to ensure these dashboards highlight student artifacts and stories, not just numbers.
  • The Local Accountability Advisory Council reviews draft visuals and helps identify what is most useful for the public.
  • These tools are steadily replacing one time, high stakes snapshots with a more complete story of learning and conditions.
Field insight: In Fleming County Schools, Phase 2 has been treated as a learning laboratory. Tools are built with the end in mind but refined in real classrooms, with real students and staff, before they are scaled. This approach keeps local accountability grounded in daily practice while building a system the community can trust.

Implementation Supports and Tools for Phase 2

Strategy only becomes real when leaders and teachers have practical tools to use. Implementation supports translate the ideas of Phase 2 into daily practice and make the work executable.

Core Implementation Tools

  • Design templates for Next Generation Readiness Assessments and BRIDGE Performance Assessments, including exemplar tasks and scoring guides.
  • Portfolio and BPI Website checklists that outline required artifacts, reflection prompts, and curation timelines.
  • Measures of Quality evidence collection protocols and sample evidence banks for each indicator.
  • Survey administration and reporting guides that include scripts, timelines, and sample communication to families.

Supports for Leaders and Teams

  • Implementation dashboards or trackers to monitor which schools are piloting which tools and when.
  • Facilitation guides for calibration sessions, pilot debriefs, and Local Accountability Advisory Council meetings.
  • Board and community report templates that integrate quantitative indicators with student artifacts and narratives.
  • References to toolkits and resources, such as "See Toolkit: Community Evidence Collection Protocol" or "See Template: Phase 2 Pilot Planning Guide."
Why this matters: Without practical tools, Phase 2 remains a set of good ideas. Implementation supports make it possible for districts to execute the work with consistency and to learn from that execution over time.

Research Base and References for Phase 2 Development

Phase 2 Development draws on research in implementation science, continuous improvement, and data informed decision making. These fields emphasize that sustainable change depends on clear structures, iterative testing, and authentic engagement of the people who will use new tools.

  • Bryk, A. S., Gomez, L. M., Grunow, A., & LeMahieu, P. G. (2015). Learning to Improve: How America's Schools Can Get Better at Getting Better. Highlights the importance of disciplined inquiry cycles, user centered design, and networked improvement in building effective systems.
  • Fixsen, D. L., Naoom, S. F., Blase, K. A., Friedman, R. M., & Wallace, F. (2005). Implementation Research: A Synthesis of the Literature. Identifies phases of implementation and core drivers such as training, coaching, and data systems that support high quality use of innovations in schools.
  • Fullan, M., & Quinn, J. (2016). Coherence: The Right Drivers in Action for Schools, Districts, and Systems. Argues that coherence comes from clarity of purpose, collaborative cultures, deep learning, and shared accountability structures.
  • Park, S., Hironaka, S., Carver, P., & Nordstrum, L. (2013). Continuous Improvement in Education. Describes how improvement science principles can guide the design, testing, and refinement of educational tools and systems.
  • Wayman, J. C., Cho, V., & Shaw, S. (2009). Data Systems and School Improvement: A Guide for School Leaders. Emphasizes that data systems must be designed for usability and sensemaking, not just compliance reporting, if they are to change practice.
How to use this section: These references can support conversations with boards, advisory councils, and staff about why Phase 2 emphasizes piloting, feedback cycles, and coherent system design instead of rushing to full scale implementation.
