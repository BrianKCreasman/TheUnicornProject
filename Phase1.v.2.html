Phase 1: Laying the Groundwork: Understanding Current Conditions and Readiness

Big Idea

Local Accountability Principles

Phase 1 is the truth-telling phase. Before a district builds new assessments, dashboards, or reporting routines, it has to get clear about three things: what success should mean here, what the current system is actually producing, and how ready the community is to shift from a test-centered story to a learning-centered story.

In Phase 1, the district completes a disciplined readiness scan across learning experiences, current measures, evidence quality, stakeholder trust, and implementation capacity. This work prevents the most common failure pattern in accountability redesign: building tools before the system agrees on purpose, evidence, and shared definitions.

Phase 1 outcome in one sentence: A community-owned, evidence-informed definition of student success and a realistic readiness plan that shows what to keep, what to strengthen, what to stop, and what to build next.

Design (DDIER) in Phase 1

Phase 1 sits in the Design stage of DDIER. Not design as in graphics or dashboards. Design as in psychometric and system design: defining constructs, agreeing on claims, identifying evidence, and testing whether current data can support the decisions the community wants to make.

Primary Pillars in Phase 1

Phase 1 emphasizes Pillar 1 (Purpose and Logic Model), but it also activates early work in Pillar 2 (Governance and Leadership), Pillar 3 (Community Engagement and Advocacy), and Pillar 4 (Data Validation and Utilization). Local Accountability Principles Activated in Phase 1

These principles are the guardrails. They keep Phase 1 from turning into another committee cycle, another survey cycle, or another set of disconnected metrics. In Phase 1, leaders should intentionally name which principles they are protecting and why.

  • Systemic Coherence and Outcome Alignment: define outcomes first, then align evidence, measures, and routines to those outcomes.
  • Shared Ownership and Community Co-Creation: success is defined with students, families, staff, and community, not just for them.
  • Multiple Measures and Balanced Evidence: no single test can carry the full meaning of growth, mastery, and readiness.
  • Evidence Quality and Technical Integrity: evidence must be credible enough to support the decisions the system wants to make.
  • Equity and Opportunity to Learn: interpret outcomes alongside access, supports, and learning conditions.
  • Transparency and Interpretability: stakeholders should understand what is measured, why it matters, and how to improve it.
  • Continuous Improvement and Learning Culture: Phase 1 produces a plan for iteration, not a permanent verdict.

Phase 1 Steps. What Leaders Actually Do

Phase 1 works when it is treated like a structured diagnostic, not a branding exercise. The goal is not to produce a perfect document. The goal is to produce a clear baseline and a shared definition of success that can support next-generation evidence.

Step 1: Current Conditions Scan

  • Inventory the learning experience students are currently getting across schools and grade levels. Look for patterns, not isolated anecdotes.
  • Map the existing assessment ecosystem. Include state tests, district benchmarks, classroom common assessments, performance tasks, grading practices, and portfolio evidence.
  • Identify what decisions each measure is currently used for (placement, grading, interventions, evaluation, reporting, accountability).
  • Surface the dominant story the system tells today about students (scores, compliance, growth, readiness, deficits, strengths).
  • Document constraints and non-negotiables (state requirements, graduation requirements, board priorities, staffing realities, time).

Step 2: Define Success. Draft, Test, Refine

  • Draft (or revisit) the Portrait of a Learner. Keep it tight. Traits should be observable, teachable, and assessable.
  • Translate each portrait trait into observable descriptors and examples across grade spans.
  • Test clarity with real stakeholders. Ask students and families to paraphrase what each trait means and how it shows up in real work.
  • Confirm alignment to required academic standards and graduation expectations. Alignment does not mean copy and paste. It means coherence.
  • Write the district definition of Growth, Mastery, and Readiness in plain language. Include what each looks like and what evidence can support it.

Step 3: Evidence Map and Gaps

  • Build an evidence map that links each major outcome (academic and durable skills) to evidence sources the district already has.
  • Flag evidence that is high-stakes, low-quality, or misused. Example: using a single benchmark score as a proxy for readiness.
  • Identify evidence gaps. Example: the system claims to value communication and problem solving, but has no defensible evidence for them.
  • Check opportunity-to-learn conditions where outcomes are weak. Avoid blaming students for inequitable access to strong instruction and experiences.
  • Set early priorities: what must be improved in Phase 2 because the evidence is currently insufficient for decision-making.

Step 4: Readiness and Capacity Check

  • Assess implementation readiness: leadership routines, staff understanding, collaboration time, assessment literacy, and feedback systems.
  • Assess technical readiness: scoring capacity, calibration routines, data governance, privacy, and reporting tools.
  • Assess cultural readiness: trust, clarity, and whether stakeholders believe the system will honor the full story of learning.
  • Identify risks and mitigation plans. Example: inconsistent scoring across schools, or initiative overload.
  • Commit to a realistic scope for Phase 2. The fastest path is rarely the best path if quality is not protected.

Step 5: Governance, Decision Rules, and Feedback Loops

  • Define who owns what decisions (board, superintendent, district teams, principals, teacher teams, councils).
  • Set decision rules for evidence use. Example: what evidence can support grading, placement, interventions, or public reporting.
  • Create feedback loops with transparency. Show what changed because stakeholders gave input.
  • Define the cadence of review for Phase 2 and beyond: quarterly evidence reviews, calibration cycles, and annual design revisions.
  • Write a one-page accountability narrative that explains how local evidence complements required state accountability.

Step 6: Phase 2 Build List (What We Will Develop Next)

  • Prioritize the Phase 2 build list: performance assessments, portfolios, dashboards, reporting templates, and professional learning.
  • Define minimum technical requirements for Phase 2 tools. Example: rubrics must be usable, calibratable, and interpretable.
  • Define a pilot logic: where to pilot first, what evidence will be collected, and how expansion decisions will be made.
  • Align the build list to time, staffing, and leadership routines to avoid “initiative pile-up.”
  • Publish a Phase 1 to Phase 2 transition brief for stakeholders so the next steps feel predictable and coherent.

Leader reality check: If Phase 1 does not produce an honest evidence map and a realistic readiness plan, Phase 2 becomes a tool-building phase that cannot hold up under scrutiny.

Tools, Resources, and Supports for Phase 1

Phase 1 moves faster and with less conflict when leaders bring practical tools to the table. These resources help teams do the work with clarity, coherence, and technical credibility.

Assessment Ecosystem Inventory

A simple spreadsheet that lists every assessment, its purpose, who uses it, and what decision it supports. Add a column for “evidence quality risk” (high, medium, low).

Evidence Map Template

A one-page map that links each outcome (portrait traits + priority standards) to evidence sources. Identify gaps and overused measures.

Readiness Rubric

A rubric for implementation readiness, technical readiness, and cultural readiness. Use a 1 to 4 scale with concrete descriptors for each level.

Calibration Starter Kit

A protocol for scoring consistency and shared expectations. Includes anchor papers, scoring notes, and a short norming agenda.

Stakeholder Listening Protocol

A question set for students, families, staff, and community that focuses on meaning, trust, and clarity. Include “What would make you believe this is real?”

Phase 1 Transition Brief

A plain-language, 2 to 3 page summary: what we learned, what we decided, what we will build next, and how we will report progress.

Coalition Membership

  • Students name what success feels like, not only what scores say. They also identify where the current system misses their strengths.
  • Teachers and support staff keep the portrait grounded in real learning conditions, time, and instructional realities.
  • Families connect the portrait to life beyond school and clarify what they need to trust new evidence.
  • School and district leaders ensure alignment to policy, resources, and strategic priorities so the portrait can live in daily practice.
  • Community and business partners connect readiness to local workforce needs, civic expectations, and real-world learning opportunities.
Pillar 3: Community Engagement and Advocacy Principle: Shared Ownership and Co-Creation

How the Coalition Works (with Evidence, Not Vibes)

  • Review current outcomes and evidence. Identify mismatches between what the district claims to value and what it actually measures.
  • Hold listening sessions and focus groups, then publish a “You Said, We Did” summary to build trust.
  • Cluster ideas into a small set of learner traits. Keep the set small enough to be used daily.
  • Write descriptors and examples across grade spans so the portrait is teachable and observable.
  • Stress-test the portrait with student work. Ask: “If we had to show this trait tomorrow, what evidence would we use?”

From Portrait to a Purposeful Logic Model

Six Pillars
Local Accountability Continuum
DDIER Cycle
Local Accountability Principles

Once the Portrait of a Learner is in place, the district turns it into a simple logic model that explains how daily work will help students grow toward that portrait. The logic model does not replace standards or required measures. It connects them to a bigger story about who students are becoming and what evidence can show it.

Inputs

  • Portrait of a Learner traits and descriptors.
  • Priority standards and curriculum resources.
  • Professional learning on task design and assessment literacy.
  • Collaboration time for planning and calibration.

Key Activities

  • Designing vibrant learning experiences with student voice and real-world application.
  • Building performance tasks and common rubrics tied to portrait traits and standards.
  • Using reflection protocols that make thinking visible.
  • Calibrating expectations with student work and scoring routines.

Outputs

  • Student work samples and performance evidence aligned to outcomes.
  • Student reflections and goal-setting artifacts.
  • Rubrics, anchors, and exemplars for consistent interpretation.
  • Evidence maps and data use routines for teams.

Outcomes

  • Students can explain what they are learning and why it matters.
  • Visible growth in durable skills and academic mastery.
  • Stronger alignment between classroom evidence and reporting.
  • A community-owned definition of success supported by credible evidence.

Bringing Phase 1 Design to Life in Daily Practice

How Phase 1 Guides Future Work

Six Pillars
Local Accountability Continuum
DDIER Cycle
Local Accountability Principles

When Phase 1 is done well, the Portrait of a Learner, the definitions of Growth-Mastery-Readiness, and the logic model become the reference point for every decision that follows. They shape what gets built in Phase 2, how schools implement instruction and assessment, how leaders evaluate progress, and how the community interprets reporting.

In practical terms, this means performance tasks, local measures, dashboards, and reports are all checked against the portrait and the evidence map. If a measure cannot be explained, cannot be trusted, or cannot be improved through action, it is reconsidered or redesigned. This is how you reduce clutter and protect coherence.

Phase 1 design check: Ask any student or adult: Can you describe what success looks like here, and can you show what evidence would support it. If the answer is yes, Phase 1 is anchoring the work.

Evidence, Guiding Questions, and Reflection for Phase 1

To keep Phase 1 useful and honest, districts need visible proof of clarity and readiness. The pieces below help teams move from intention to impact. Evidence focuses on what can be documented. Guiding questions steer next steps. Reflection keeps leaders and staff learning as the system shifts.

Evidence of Efficacy

  • The Portrait of a Learner, Growth-Mastery-Readiness definitions, and logic model show up in leadership routines and school plans, not just launch documents.
  • Unit plans and performance tasks explicitly connect standards to portrait traits and the district definition of readiness.
  • Student work (including portfolios) includes evidence of durable skills plus academic mastery, not only test prep artifacts.
  • Stakeholder feedback shows increasing clarity about what “counts” as evidence and why it matters.
  • The district can name what evidence is currently weak, and has a plan to improve it in Phase 2.
Pillar 4: Data Validation and Utilization Principle: Evidence Quality

Guiding Questions

  • Where is the portrait visible in actual student work, and where is it still mostly words on paper.
  • Which current measures are being used for decisions they cannot defensibly support.
  • Do our reports help people take action, or do they just label.
  • Where do we see opportunity-to-learn gaps that must be addressed before interpreting outcomes.
  • What is the smallest Phase 2 build that would meaningfully improve evidence quality.
Continuum: Define Success for Students Principle: Equity and Opportunity to Learn Principle: Continuous Improvement

Reflection

  • As a district leader, where does the current evidence support confident decisions, and where are we guessing.
  • As a principal, what routines will you protect to keep coherence (planning, calibration, evidence reviews).
  • As a teacher, what task or assessment would best show a student’s growth and readiness that a test score cannot show.
  • As a family member, what would make reporting feel more honest, more useful, and more connected to learning.
  • What is one practice we should stop because it produces noise, not clarity.
Use in practice: Build these prompts into leadership agendas and school team meetings so Phase 1 stays alive while Phase 2 begins.

Stakeholder Engagement, Empowerment, and Reciprocity in Phase 1

Local accountability becomes real when every stakeholder group has a visible role in Phase 1. Engagement is participation. Empowerment is influence. Reciprocity is shared responsibility and shared benefit. In this phase, each group helps define success, review evidence, and shape the readiness plan so the system reflects lived experience, not just central office design.

School and District Leaders

  • Convene a representative coalition to co-create definitions and evidence expectations.
  • Share data and constraints transparently so stakeholders can contribute with context.
  • Publish decision logs (what we decided, why we decided it, and what evidence informed it).
  • Protect coherence by stopping low-value measures and routines that create noise.
Pillar 2: Governance and Leadership Principle: Systemic Coherence

Teachers and School Staff

  • Translate drafts into sample tasks and evidence examples to test usability.
  • Bring student work back to the coalition so real evidence shapes decisions.
  • Identify what supports are needed (planning time, calibration, rubrics, exemplars).
  • Help students connect learning targets to portrait traits through reflection.
Pillar 4: Data Validation and Utilization Principle: Evidence Quality

Parents and Guardians

  • Participate in listening sessions focused on what they need to trust evidence beyond test scores.
  • Share strengths and aspirations to keep definitions of success human and local.
  • React to draft portrait language and sample evidence (portfolios, performance work, reflections).
  • Help refine reporting expectations so information becomes actionable, not confusing.
Pillar 3: Community Engagement and Advocacy Principle: Transparency

Students

  • Identify where the current system misses their learning, skills, and talents.
  • Provide work samples that demonstrate growth and readiness in ways tests cannot.
  • Help refine reflection prompts and portfolio expectations for authenticity.
  • Practice self-assessment and goal-setting using the portrait language.

Community and Partners

  • Connect readiness definitions to local workforce, civic, and community needs.
  • Offer real-world challenges that can become performance tasks later.
  • Review evidence expectations and ask whether they reflect what matters after graduation.
  • Serve as accountability partners by asking how the design advances equity and opportunity.

Best Practices Across Stakeholders

  • Use transparent feedback loops and publish what changed because of input.
  • Review evidence together, not in separate silos, so everyone sees the same story.
  • Use plain language. If stakeholders cannot interpret it, it will not sustain.
  • Define “how we will improve” alongside “how we will measure,” every time.
Why this matters: When Phase 1 includes real influence and reciprocity, local accountability becomes something districts do with their community, not to them. That shared ownership is the strongest predictor of long-term implementation.

Phase 1: Key Outcome

By the end of Phase 1, the district should have more than a vision statement. It should have a small set of concrete products that anchor local accountability and create a strong runway into Phase 2. These outcomes make it possible to build coherent tools, instead of bolting accountability onto existing work.

1) Shared Definition of Student Success

  • A finalized Portrait of a Learner created with students, staff, families, and community partners.
  • Plain-language definitions of Growth, Mastery, and Readiness, including what evidence can support each claim.
  • Examples of what each portrait trait looks like in real student work across grade spans.
  • A short narrative that explains success in this district in a way any stakeholder can repeat.

Practice in Action: Phase 1 in Fleming County Schools

Fleming County Schools has treated Phase 1 as more than planning. It has been the foundation for a shift from a test-centered story to a student-evidence story. The work connects the Portrait of a Learner, BRIDGE Performance Indicators, Measures of Quality, Next-Generation Readiness Assessments, and BPI Websites into one coherent definition of success.

Defining Success for Students

  • The Portrait of a Learner names durable skills that matter in Fleming County, such as communication, collaboration, problem solving, and real-world application.
  • BRIDGE Performance Indicators translate those outcomes into evidence that can be curated and communicated over time.
  • Stakeholder groups refine definitions using community voice so the system reflects local values.
  • The story of success centers on who students are becoming and what they can do with learning, not only what they score.
Pillar 1: Purpose and Logic Model Principle: Shared Ownership Principle: Multiple Measures

Evidence Map in Action

  • Measures of Quality 4.1 functions as an organizing logic that links learning experiences, culture, community connection, and ecosystem supports.
  • Next-Generation Readiness Assessments create structured opportunities for students to demonstrate mastery and durable skills through real tasks.
  • BPI Websites act as living portfolios where students curate evidence of growth, mastery, and readiness across time.
  • Evidence is treated as improvable. If it is weak, the response is better tasks, better rubrics, and better calibration, not blame.
Continuum: Demonstrate Student Success Principle: Evidence Quality Principle: Continuous Improvement

Design Choices that Protect Coherence

  • Student agency and real-world application are treated as required design features of success, not optional add-ons.
  • Dashboards and reports are built after outcomes and evidence claims are clear, so reporting reflects learning rather than driving it.
  • Phase 1 is revisited through evidence reviews and stakeholder loops so design remains responsive, not fixed.
  • The district maintains a balanced story of Growth, Mastery, and Readiness to avoid collapsing success into a single number.

What Phase 1 Looks Like to Stakeholders

  • Students see themselves named in success criteria and have a place to show evidence beyond test scores.
  • Teachers have clearer targets for what counts as readiness and better tools for capturing it.
  • Families can see how evidence connects to learning experiences, not just outcomes.
  • Board members receive information that supports governance focused on learning, culture, and readiness.
Field insight: In Fleming County Schools, Phase 1 is not a binder on a shelf. It is a living reference point that keeps local accountability centered on vibrant learning and credible student evidence.
