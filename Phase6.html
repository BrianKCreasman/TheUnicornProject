Phase 6 Overview

Phase 6: Continuous Improvement and Networked Learning focuses on turning the local accountability system into a living learning engine. The central question becomes, How do we use what we are learning to keep improving practice, strengthening equity, and influencing the wider educational ecosystem.

On the Local Accountability Continuum, Phase 6 corresponds to Deepen, Learn, and Share. In the DDIER cycle, Phase 6 is where Evaluation and Revision connect directly back to a new round of Design and Development. The system does not reset. It loops forward.

Phase 6 draws strongly on Pillar 4: Data Validation, Analysis, and Utilization, Pillar 5: Evaluating Progress, Impact, and Reporting, and Pillar 6: Sustainability, Partnerships, and Innovation. These pillars help the district move from proving to improving, and from improving to influencing.

Why this matters: Without a deliberate continuous improvement phase, local accountability can become a static structure. Phase 6 keeps it dynamic, responsive, and connected to broader learning networks.

Current On the Ground Conditions and Self Assessment of Context

Phase 6 begins with a clear-eyed look at how the system is learning now. Districts consider where continuous improvement is already part of daily practice, and where it needs to be strengthened.

Profiles of Improvement Culture

  • Compliance Focused: Data is collected mainly to satisfy external requirements.
  • Mixed Practice: Some teams use evidence for improvement, while others focus on reporting only.
  • Learning Centered: The system routinely uses evidence to ask questions, test changes, and improve practice.

Context Questions for Phase 6

  • Where are improvement cycles already happening, and how formal or informal are they.
  • How do teams currently respond when local accountability evidence reveals gaps or inequities.
  • What routines support reflection and adjustment across classrooms, schools, and the district.
  • How connected is the district to external learning networks that can deepen and extend its work.
Why this matters: Continuous improvement strategies land best in systems that already treat evidence as a tool for learning rather than judgment.

Guiding Principles for Continuous Improvement and Networked Learning

Guiding principles keep Phase 6 grounded in equity, learning, and coherence. They help districts resist the pull toward quick fixes and one time responses.

Principles for Continuous Improvement

  • Center students, especially those furthest from opportunity, in all improvement questions.
  • Use evidence to learn and adjust rather than to sort or blame.
  • Focus on small, disciplined tests of change that build toward larger shifts.
  • Make improvement work visible so it can be understood, supported, and refined.

Principles for Networked Learning

  • Share tools, findings, and stories so others can learn from local accountability work.
  • Learn from other districts, organizations, and networks that are pursuing similar aims.
  • Adapt ideas thoughtfully rather than copying them wholesale.
  • Use networks to strengthen, not dilute, the district's vision for Growth, Mastery, and Readiness.
Why this matters: Clear principles ensure that continuous improvement does not become a scatter of unrelated projects, but a coherent way of working together.

Preconditions and Readiness Indicators for Phase 6

Certain conditions help Phase 6 succeed. These preconditions signal that the system is ready to deepen its learning posture and share its work more broadly.

Minimum Criteria for Productive Entry

  • Phases 3 through 5 have generated multiple cycles of implementation, evaluation, and revision.
  • Core components of local accountability are in place and functioning across the system.
  • There is a basic culture of reflection, feedback, and adaptation within leadership and staff teams.
  • District leaders are ready to invest time in structured improvement cycles and network participation.

Readiness Indicators to Check

  • Teachers and leaders have experience reviewing local evidence in PLCs and leadership teams.
  • There are existing examples of improvements that grew out of local accountability findings.
  • Staff show interest in learning from and contributing to broader networks.
  • Board and community partners recognize the value of improvement oriented conversations.
Why this matters: These indicators help districts avoid treating continuous improvement as a slogan, and instead build on real, existing strengths.

Designing the Improvement Architecture

Continuous improvement is easier to sustain when there is an architecture that supports it. Phase 6 clarifies how teams, data, time, and networks connect.

Improvement Teams

  • Cross-functional teams that include teachers, leaders, and support staff.
  • Standing teams that meet regularly to review evidence and test changes.
  • Clear roles for facilitation, data analysis, and documentation.
  • Student and family participation where appropriate and feasible.

Data and Learning Routines

  • Standard routines for examining NxGRA results, BPI artifacts, Measures of Quality, and survey data.
  • Protocols for asking why patterns exist, not only what the numbers show.
  • Use of simple visual tools that make trends and gaps visible.
  • Shared agreements about how to discuss data in a way that promotes trust and learning.

Time and Structures

  • Protected time in PLCs and leadership meetings for improvement cycles.
  • Annual calendars that map when evidence is collected, studied, and used to inform changes.
  • Alignment between improvement work and other required planning processes.
  • Flexible space for design labs or cross-school improvement efforts.

Networks and Partnerships

  • Connections to regional, state, or national networks focused on local accountability and assessment.
  • Partnerships with universities and research centers that support improvement work.
  • Collaborative learning communities with other districts sharing tools and lessons.
  • Agreements that support joint inquiry, not only compliance or external evaluation.
Improvement anchor: When teams, routines, time, and networks are aligned, continuous improvement becomes a habit rather than an exception.

Core Continuous Improvement Workstreams

Workstreams in Phase 6 give structure to improvement efforts. They help the district focus on a small set of big questions each year.

Workstream 1: Improving Vibrant Learning Experiences

  • Use local evidence to identify gaps in access to vibrant learning across classrooms and schools.
  • Design and test changes in instruction, assessment, and student supports to close those gaps.
  • Examine student artifacts and feedback to see whether changes are improving relevance and challenge.
  • Scale successful practices while refining or removing those that do not add value.

Workstream 2: Strengthening Measures and Reporting

  • Review Measures of Quality, dashboards, and reports for clarity, fairness, and usefulness.
  • Test new visualizations or narratives that better communicate student growth and readiness.
  • Gather feedback from students, families, and staff about what is most helpful for decision making.
  • Revise measures and reporting formats based on what the evidence and feedback reveal.

Workstream 3: Equity and Access

  • Use disaggregated local evidence to understand who is and is not benefiting from the system.
  • Co design strategies with students and communities to address identified inequities.
  • Monitor whether changes are closing gaps in opportunity, participation, and outcomes.
  • Feed findings directly into the next DDIER design and development cycle.

Workstream 4: Networked Learning and Influence

  • Participate in networks that are also building local accountability and performance assessment systems.
  • Share tools, examples, and lessons from the district's work.
  • Learn from other districts and adapt promising ideas in ways that fit local context.
  • Use network findings to refine the system and advocate for supportive policy conditions.
Practical tip: Districts can select one or two workstreams per year to keep the scope manageable while still driving meaningful system learning.

Progress and Impact Indicators in Phase 6

Phase 6 focuses on whether the system is becoming more effective at learning from itself and from others.

Progress Indicators for Continuous Improvement

  • Regular use of structured inquiry protocols in PLCs and leadership teams.
  • Documented examples of small tests of change and what was learned from them.
  • Increasing comfort and skill among staff in discussing data and evidence.
  • Visible adjustments to practice, tools, or supports based on local evidence.

Impact Indicators for Networked Learning

  • Participation in networks results in concrete changes to local tools and practices.
  • The district contributes resources, case studies, or tools that others find useful.
  • Evidence that students' experiences are improving as a result of shared learning.
  • Growing recognition of the district as a leader in local accountability and vibrant learning.
Why this matters: These indicators help districts see whether Phase 6 is creating a virtuous cycle of learning rather than a one time improvement push.

Evidence and Key Questions for Continuous Improvement

Phase 6 uses evidence to understand not only what is happening, but how the system is learning over time.

Evidence to Monitor

  • Documentation of improvement cycles, including change ideas, tests, and outcomes.
  • Patterns in how often and how deeply teams engage with local accountability evidence.
  • Longitudinal data on student growth, mastery, readiness, and engagement.
  • Shifts in educator beliefs and practices related to assessment, instruction, and accountability.
  • Participation levels and feedback from network partners and collaborative efforts.

Key Questions

  • Where are improvement cycles leading to real changes in student experience.
  • Where do we see repeated challenges that are not yet improving, and why.
  • What have we learned about equity that should shape the next design cycle.
  • How are we capturing and sharing learning so it does not live in isolated pockets.
  • What network experiences have most influenced our thinking and practice, and how.

Learning Loop: Closing One Cycle and Opening the Next

  • What three to five key lessons from this cycle must shape our next round of design and development.
  • Which components of the local accountability system should remain stable, and which should be redesigned.
  • How will we involve students, staff, families, and partners in co designing the next cycle.
  • What capacity building is needed so more people can participate in improvement work.
  • How will we tell the story of this cycle in a way that honors both progress and unfinished work.
Use in practice: These questions can guide an annual reflection that formally closes one DDIER cycle and launches the next.

Stakeholder Roles in Phase 6 Continuous Improvement and Networked Learning

Phase 6 clarifies how each stakeholder group contributes to a culture of ongoing learning.

Superintendent and Cabinet

  • Set expectations that local evidence will be used to drive improvement, not only to report results.
  • Allocate time and resources for structured improvement cycles.
  • Engage in networked learning with peers and bring insights back to the district.
  • Model openness about what is working well and what still needs to improve.

Board of Education

  • Ask improvement focused questions when reviewing local accountability evidence.
  • Support strategic investments in capacity building and network participation.
  • Celebrate both accomplishments and honest reflection about challenges.
  • Collaborate with district leaders to communicate the learning journey to the community.

District and School Leaders

  • Facilitate improvement teams and ensure routines are followed consistently.
  • Use Phase 6 findings to adjust supports, schedules, and expectations.
  • Encourage staff to bring forward ideas for change and test them in manageable ways.
  • Share learning across schools to avoid isolated pockets of improvement.

Teachers and Staff

  • Participate actively in improvement cycles and networked learning opportunities.
  • Use local evidence to reflect on and refine classroom practice.
  • Share examples of student work and experiences that illustrate progress and need.
  • Co design and test change ideas that are grounded in student voice and data.

Students, Families, and Community Partners

  • Provide feedback about how local accountability is shaping student experience.
  • Participate in co design conversations about improvements and new ideas.
  • Share community perspectives that deepen understanding of local context and needs.
  • Support and advocate for changes that improve learning and readiness for all students.

Best Practices Across Stakeholders

  • Create regular opportunities to share learning across roles and levels.
  • Use inclusive processes that invite diverse voices into improvement work.
  • Document and celebrate small wins to sustain momentum.
  • Keep the focus on students and learning as the reason for every change idea.
Why this matters: When all stakeholders see themselves as co learners, continuous improvement becomes a shared culture rather than a technical exercise.

Risks and Mitigation Strategies in Phase 6

Continuous improvement work carries its own risks. Anticipating them helps keep the work focused and sustaining.

Risk: Improvement Overload

  • Staff feel overwhelmed by too many improvement projects at once.
  • Mitigation: Limit the number of active workstreams and focus on a few high leverage questions per year.
  • Mitigation: Retire or merge initiatives that are no longer needed.

Risk: Surface Level Changes

  • Improvement efforts focus on easy adjustments rather than root causes.
  • Mitigation: Use structured inquiry tools that prompt deeper analysis.
  • Mitigation: Include diverse perspectives, especially from students and families, in problem framing.

Risk: Network Drift

  • Participation in networks leads to adopting ideas that do not fit local context.
  • Mitigation: Use the Portrait of a Learner and core principles as filters for what to adopt.
  • Mitigation: Test borrowed ideas in small pilots before scaling.

Risk: Data Fatigue

  • Teams become exhausted by constant data collection and analysis.
  • Mitigation: Streamline data sources and focus on a manageable set of indicators.
  • Mitigation: Emphasize meaning making and action over volume.
Why this matters: Addressing these risks directly helps the district keep Phase 6 grounded, humane, and sustainable.

Phase 6: Key Outcome

By the end of Phase 6, the district should have a coherent, documented continuous improvement and networked learning practice that is fully integrated into its local accountability system and ready to inform the next DDIER cycle.

System Level Outcome

  • Improvement routines are embedded in PLCs, leadership meetings, and district planning processes.
  • There is a clear record of what has been learned from local accountability evidence over multiple years.
  • Staff at all levels can describe how the system learns and adjusts over time.
  • Network participation is purposeful and aligned to district priorities.

Readiness for the Next Cycle

  • A concise set of improvement priorities for the next DDIER cycle, grounded in Phase 6 learning.
  • Agreements about which practices and tools will remain stable while new designs are tested.
  • Plans for capacity building to support deeper involvement in improvement work.
  • A shared understanding that local accountability is a continuous learning journey, not a static framework.
Why this matters: When Phase 6 concludes with a living improvement practice and clear next steps, the system can keep evolving without losing its identity.

Practice in Action: Phase 6 in Fleming County Schools

In Fleming County Schools, Phase 6 means treating Vibrant Accountability as a learning lab, not a finished product. The district uses what it is learning to refine tools, strengthen equity, and share its work with others.

Continuous Improvement in Daily Practice

  • PLC and leadership agendas regularly include time for studying local evidence and testing changes.
  • Teachers and leaders use student artifacts, NxGRA results, and Measures of Quality to identify specific improvement ideas.
  • Small tests of change are documented and shared across schools.
  • Successes and challenges from improvement efforts shape the next round of design and development.

Networked Learning and Influence

  • Fleming County contributes tools, stories, and findings to state and national networks.
  • District teams learn from other local accountability efforts and adapt ideas to fit the FCS context.
  • Partnerships with organizations such as universities and research centers support deeper inquiry.
  • Vibrant Accountability is recognized as both a local system and a contribution to the broader field.
Field insight: Treating Phase 6 as a standing practice, not a one time stage, helps Fleming County keep its local accountability system vibrant and evolving.

Implementation Supports and Tools for Phase 6

Continuous improvement and networked learning require practical tools that help teams work in disciplined, efficient ways.

Core Improvement Tools

  • Inquiry protocols for PLCs and leadership teams that structure evidence review and change ideas.
  • Templates for documenting small tests of change and their results.
  • Annual improvement maps that align local accountability evidence with key questions and workstreams.
  • Guidebooks such as "See Toolkit: Local Accountability Improvement Cycles in Practice."

Supports for Networked Learning

  • Tools for capturing and sharing learning from conferences, visits, and network meetings.
  • Resources for presenting district work to external audiences in clear and accessible ways.
  • Planning templates for reciprocal visits and joint inquiry with other districts.
  • References to resources such as "See Guide: Joining and Leading Local Accountability Networks."
Why this matters: Tools and supports make it possible for improvement and networked learning to be part of the regular work of educators, not an extra burden.
