Phase 3 Overview

Phase 3: Implementation is where the local accountability system moves from design and pilots to everyday practice. The core question becomes, Are we using the tools we built in ways that improve teaching, learning, and decision making across the system. The work shifts from building tools to building habits, routines, and culture around those tools.

On the Local Accountability Continuum, Phase 3 corresponds to Implement the Local Accountability System. In the DDIER cycle, it aligns with the Implementation phase, where the system blueprint from Phase 2 is put into motion across more schools, grade levels, and teams. The focus is on consistent use, not perfection.

Phase 3 is especially anchored in Pillar 2: Governance, Leadership, and Systematic Support and Pillar 4: Data Validation, Analysis, and Utilization, with growing attention to how progress is reported, communicated, and sustained over time.

Why this matters: A local accountability system only becomes real when teachers, leaders, students, and communities use it in daily decisions. Phase 3 tests whether the system can stand on its own, beyond the design team and early pilots.

Current On the Ground Conditions and Self Assessment of Context

Before fully engaging in Phase 3, districts take an honest look at what implementation actually looks like now. The goal is to describe lived reality, not the implementation plan. Self assessment profiles help teams locate themselves and tailor the pace and supports for this phase.

Profiles of Current Practice

  • Patchy Implementation: A few strong pilot sites are using local tools, but most schools still rely primarily on traditional tests and grades.
  • Expanding but Fragile: Multiple schools use NxGRAs, portfolios, Measures of Quality, and surveys, but routines are inconsistent and dependent on a few key people.
  • Emerging System: Local accountability tools appear in school improvement plans, leadership meetings, and board conversations, but calibration and coherence still need work.

Using Context to Shape Phase 3

  • Align early scaling to the schools and teams most ready to lead by example.
  • Identify schools or grade levels where legacy practices may slow or distort implementation.
  • Surface differences in capacity and trust so supports can be differentiated, not one size fits all.
  • Share a short narrative of “how things really work now” with advisory groups and the board to ground expectations.
Why this matters: When Phase 3 starts with a clear picture of current conditions, districts can scale what is working, support what is struggling, and avoid pushing a fragile system beyond its capacity.

Guiding Principles for Phase 3 Implementation

Guiding principles keep Phase 3 anchored to the district’s beliefs about learning, evidence, and equity. They offer a compass when leaders face tradeoffs between speed, depth, and sustainability.

Principles for Learning and Evidence

  • Implementation must protect instructional quality, not simply add compliance tasks to teacher workload.
  • Evidence routines should make Growth, Mastery, and Readiness more visible, not add more noise to a crowded system.
  • Students should understand how NxGRAs, portfolios, and Measures of Quality help them see and tell the story of their learning.
  • Local evidence should elevate student voice and real world performance, not only reinforce existing test score patterns.

Principles for Pace and Support

  • Prioritize depth before breadth, scaling a few high leverage tools well instead of many tools superficially.
  • Successful implementation is measured by the typical school, not only by early adopter sites.
  • Every new expectation must be paired with time, examples, coaching, and chances to practice in community.
  • Feedback from schools and stakeholders is treated as fuel for improvement, not resistance to be dismissed.
Why this matters: Without clear principles, Phase 3 can slide into “initiative push” instead of thoughtful implementation, putting the entire local accountability vision at risk.

Preconditions and Readiness Indicators for Phase Entry

Phase 3 should not begin until certain conditions are in place. These preconditions ensure that implementation will be meaningful and sustainable, not rushed and fragile.

Minimum Criteria for Productive Entry

  • Phase 2 tools (NxGRAs, portfolio expectations, Measures of Quality, surveys, dashboards) exist in version 1.0 and have been piloted and revised at least once.
  • A written implementation plan identifies which tools will scale first, in which schools or grades, and on what timeline.
  • Principals and teacher leaders can explain the purpose of local accountability and how Phase 3 connects to Phase 1 and Phase 2 decisions.
  • The board and advisory councils have been briefed on what will look different in practice as Phase 3 unfolds.

Readiness Indicators to Check

  • Pilot teachers are willing to recommend the new tools to colleagues and support early implementation at their sites.
  • At least some school improvement plans already reference local evidence such as BPI websites, NxGRAs, and Measures of Quality.
  • Professional learning calendars include explicit time for calibration, scoring, and reflection on local evidence.
  • Communication routines exist for sharing updates with staff, students, families, and community partners.
Why this matters: When districts enter Phase 3 with clear preconditions and readiness indicators, they reduce the risk of implementation being experienced as one more disconnected initiative.

Putting the Blueprint in Motion

In Phase 2, districts built a system blueprint that connected classroom assessments, school level measures, district indicators, and public reporting. In Phase 3, the question becomes, Can this blueprint be lived without the design team in the room.

Annual Accountability Calendar

  • Schedules for NxGRAs and key performance assessments across grades and subjects.
  • Regular checkpoints for BPI curation and portfolio reflection.
  • Windows for Measures of Quality evidence collection and scoring.
  • Survey administration periods and dashboard update cycles.

Decision Routines

  • Principal and leadership team meetings that use local evidence alongside state data.
  • School improvement cycles that include NxGRAs, BPI artifacts, and Measures of Quality ratings.
  • Cabinet and board discussions that reference Growth, Mastery, Readiness, and Measures of Quality.
  • Student and family conferences that use portfolio evidence to discuss progress and goals.

Feedback and Support Structures

  • PLC time dedicated to reviewing student work and calibrating scoring.
  • Coaching cycles focused on performance assessment, portfolio use, and data conversations.
  • Advisory councils that periodically review implementation evidence and recommend adjustments.
  • Communication routines that keep staff, students, and families informed about what is being implemented and why.

Monitoring and Adjustment

  • Simple dashboards that track where tools are being used and where support is needed.
  • Implementation check-ins at leadership meetings tied to specific milestones.
  • Processes to retire or refine tools that are not adding value.
  • Clear documentation of lessons learned to inform future phases of work.
Implementation anchor: A strong Phase 3 architecture makes it easy to see when tools are in use, how they are being used, and where the system needs more support, not more initiatives.

Core Implementation Workstreams

Implementation workstreams in Phase 3 mirror the design workstreams from Phase 2, but the emphasis shifts from building tools to using them well and consistently.

Workstream 1: NxGRAs and Portfolios in Use

  • Scale Next Generation Readiness Assessments into additional grades and subjects according to the implementation plan.
  • Establish minimum expectations for BPI and portfolio updates, such as curated artifacts and reflections each term.
  • Host calibration sessions where teachers score student work together and refine interpretations of rubrics.
  • Support students in understanding how artifacts show their Growth, Mastery, and Readiness.

Workstream 2: Measures of Quality in School Improvement

  • Require each school to complete Measures of Quality ratings with documented evidence on a predictable schedule.
  • Integrate results into school improvement plans, principal supervision, and leadership coaching.
  • Look for cross school patterns to identify bright spots and common areas for growth.
  • Use Measures of Quality to connect Vibrant Learning, Outcomes, Culture, Community, and Ecosystem in one narrative.

Workstream 3: Surveys, Culture, and Climate

  • Implement student, staff, and family surveys on a predictable, transparent schedule.
  • Share results in plain language visuals, along with short summaries of key insights.
  • Co design follow up actions with school teams so survey data leads to visible change.
  • Track culture and climate trends over time and connect them to instructional and leadership practices.

Workstream 4: Dashboards and Public Reporting

  • Move from prototype dashboards to usable leader dashboards that integrate multiple measures.
  • Develop public facing reports that highlight student artifacts, stories, and indicators in accessible formats.
  • Train principals and district leaders to use dashboards in conversations with staff, students, families, and the board.
  • Refine dashboards based on feedback about clarity, usefulness, and alignment to community priorities.
Practical tip: Districts do not need a different team for every workstream. The same core coalition can rotate focus across the year, as long as priorities are clear and timelines are realistic.

Milestones and Progress Indicators for Phase 3

Milestones in Phase 3 are about implementation quality and early impact, not perfection. They help districts ask, “Are we on track to build stable patterns of practice.”

Implementation Milestones

  • A defined percentage of schools administer NxGRAs and capture resulting artifacts in student BPI websites.
  • All schools complete Measures of Quality ratings with documented evidence by agreed upon deadlines.
  • Survey response rates reach minimum thresholds for students, staff, and families.
  • Dashboards and reports are updated and used according to the annual accountability calendar.

Behavioral and Cultural Indicators

  • Leadership teams reference local evidence, not just state test data, in recurring meetings.
  • Teachers use portfolio evidence in student and family conferences to discuss progress and next steps.
  • Board members ask questions about Growth, Mastery, Readiness, and Measures of Quality when reviewing district progress.
  • Students can explain how their BPI websites and performance assessments show who they are as learners.
Why this matters: These milestones help districts see Phase 3 as more than a rollout checklist. They signal whether the system is beginning to change daily conversations and decisions about learning.

Evidence and Key Questions in Implementation

In Phase 2, evidence focused on tool quality and usability. In Phase 3, evidence shifts toward implementation quality and early effects on practice and culture.

Evidence to Monitor

  • Implementation logs that track which schools are using which tools, and to what extent.
  • Samples of scored student work and portfolios from multiple schools and grade levels.
  • Measures of Quality results by school, with accompanying narrative explanations.
  • Survey trends over time for students, staff, and families.
  • Examples of decisions (schedules, PD, resource allocation) that explicitly reference local evidence.

Key Questions for Sensemaking

  • Are tools being used as they were designed, or have they drifted in ways that reduce their impact.
  • Where is implementation deepest, and what conditions make that possible in those schools or teams.
  • Where is implementation most fragile, and what barriers are appearing repeatedly across sites.
  • Are teachers and leaders reporting that the system helps them see students more clearly and make better decisions.
  • Are conversations with families and the board showing a richer story of learning than before Phase 3.

Learning Loop: Reflection and Next Steps

  • Where did we see the most meaningful improvements in clarity, coherence, and student evidence during Phase 3.
  • Which tools proved most powerful for changing practice and which added complexity with limited value.
  • How are students and families describing the system, and what language are they using to talk about Growth, Mastery, and Readiness.
  • What do we need to refine, simplify, or strengthen before scaling to all schools or all grade levels.
  • How will our learning from Phase 3 shape the focus of the next phase of local accountability work.
Use in practice: Embed these reflection questions into end of year reviews, leadership retreats, and advisory council meetings so Phase 3 becomes a learning cycle, not only an implementation cycle.

Stakeholder Roles in Phase 3 Implementation

In Phase 3, roles shift from design and pilot work to ownership and refinement. Each group contributes to making the system real in everyday practice.

Superintendent and Cabinet

  • Use local evidence in internal and public messaging to signal its importance and legitimacy.
  • Protect time for schools to engage in calibration, reflection, and portfolio curation.
  • Align district initiatives and PD priorities with the local accountability vision.
  • Hold the line when pressures arise to revert to test only narratives about school performance.

Principals and School Leaders

  • Integrate local accountability tools into faculty meetings, PLCs, and school improvement processes.
  • Support teachers in embedding NxGRAs and portfolio work into instruction, not as add ons.
  • Use Measures of Quality, survey results, and student artifacts to guide school decisions.
  • Communicate regularly with students and families about how local evidence is being used.

Teachers

  • Administer NxGRAs and performance tasks in ways that feel integrated with daily teaching.
  • Support students in curating BPI websites and reflecting on their learning across time.
  • Engage in calibration sessions and use insights to refine instruction and assessment.
  • Provide feedback on what works, what does not, and what needs adjustment in the tools or expectations.

Students

  • Curate artifacts and reflections in BPI websites that show Growth, Mastery, and Readiness.
  • Participate in NxGRAs, portfolios, and surveys and offer feedback on relevance and challenge.
  • Use local evidence to set goals, track progress, and communicate their learning story.
  • Serve as ambassadors who can explain the local accountability system in student language.

Families and Community Partners

  • Review dashboards, artifacts, and reports and respond with questions, priorities, and ideas.
  • Participate in conferences, showcases, and community events that highlight student work.
  • Serve on advisory councils or focus groups that review local evidence and suggest refinements.
  • Help identify real world contexts and opportunities that deepen performance assessment tasks.

Best Practices Across Stakeholders

  • Use structured protocols for reviewing evidence together so discussions stay focused and productive.
  • Share clear “you said, we did” examples so stakeholders see how their input shapes implementation.
  • Recognize and celebrate schools, teams, and students who model the spirit of local accountability.
  • Keep returning to the core question, how does this system help more students experience real learning and real accomplishment.
Why this matters: When stakeholders see themselves as co owners of the local accountability system, not just participants, trust and sustainability grow.

Risks and Mitigation Strategies in Phase 3

Implementation brings real world constraints into view. Naming risks and planning mitigation strategies helps districts navigate complexity with intention rather than surprise.

Risk: Reversion to Old Habits

  • Schools default to traditional tests and grades when time pressures or anxiety spike.
  • Mitigation: Continually elevate concrete stories where local evidence improved outcomes or decisions.
  • Mitigation: Integrate local accountability tools into required processes, not only optional practices.

Risk: Implementation Fatigue

  • Staff experience the work as one more initiative layered on top of many others.
  • Mitigation: Tighten focus to the “critical few” tools and intentionally sunset lower value initiatives.
  • Mitigation: Use staff feedback to simplify processes and reduce duplication.

Risk: Inconsistent Use Across Schools

  • Some schools implement deeply while others barely adopt the tools, leading to uneven experiences for students.
  • Mitigation: Use implementation data to identify bright spots and create cross school learning structures.
  • Mitigation: Pair schools or teams so those further along can coach and support those just starting.

Risk: Data Without Action

  • Dashboards, Measures of Quality, and surveys are completed but do not lead to visible change.
  • Mitigation: Build simple “Now what” protocols into every data conversation.
  • Mitigation: Track and share examples of actions taken based on local evidence to reinforce expectations.
Why this matters: Planning for risks keeps Phase 3 from becoming reactive and helps leaders stay focused on building a resilient, learning oriented system.

Phase 3: Key Outcome

By the end of Phase 3, the district should have a functional local accountability system in use across schools, with consistent routines for collecting, scoring, and using multiple forms of evidence to inform decisions at classroom, school, and district levels.

System in Use

  • NxGRAs, portfolios/BPI websites, Measures of Quality, surveys, and dashboards are in regular use in defined grades and schools.
  • Teachers, leaders, students, and families can describe how these tools show learning beyond test scores.
  • Local evidence is visible in school improvement plans, leadership conversations, and board materials.
  • Implementation patterns are stable enough that they can be studied and improved, not rebuilt from scratch.

Readiness for the Next Phase

  • Districts have identified where the system is strongest and where it needs targeted refinement.
  • There is agreement about which tools and routines will move to full scale and which remain in refinement.
  • Leaders have a clear picture of the supports needed to sustain and deepen implementation.
  • The system is ready to enter a phase focused on validation, refinement, and long term sustainability.
Why this matters: When these outcomes are present, Phase 4 can focus on improving and sustaining a real system, not on building one from scratch again.

Practice in Action: Phase 3 in Fleming County Schools

In Fleming County Schools, Phase 3 represents the move from pilot projects to a lived local accountability system woven into everyday practice. The emphasis is on making Growth, Mastery, and Readiness visible for every student, in every school, through multiple forms of evidence.

NxGRAs and BPI Websites at Scale

  • Students in grades 4–12 complete at least one Next Generation Readiness Assessment each term, with artifacts curated in their BPI websites.
  • Teachers use common rubrics that connect standards, durable skills, and real world application to guide scoring and feedback.
  • Student led conferences and celebrations of learning feature BPI artifacts and reflections as primary evidence.
  • Leaders reference BPI websites as key sources of insight when discussing student readiness.

Measures of Quality and Community Conversation

  • All schools complete Measures of Quality 4.1 with documented evidence in Vibrant Learning, Outcomes, Culture, Community, and Ecosystem.
  • Results inform leadership coaching and school improvement strategies, not only annual reports.
  • Community and advisory groups review Measures of Quality alongside student work and surveys.
  • Board updates increasingly highlight local measures as a core part of how success is defined and communicated.
Field insight: Treating Phase 3 as a learning laboratory, not a compliance exercise, allows Fleming County to refine tools in real use and build a system that is both rigorous and human.

Implementation Supports and Tools for Phase 3

Implementation supports make the work executable for busy educators and leaders. They translate strategy into daily practice.

Core Implementation Tools

  • Annual local accountability calendar templates that integrate NxGRAs, portfolios, Measures of Quality, surveys, and reporting.
  • PLC and staff meeting protocols for reviewing student work, Measures of Quality evidence, and survey data.
  • Scoring and calibration guides for NxGRAs and portfolio artifacts.
  • Communication templates for explaining Phase 3 implementation to staff, students, families, and community partners.

Supports for Leaders and Teams

  • Implementation dashboards or trackers that show where tools are in use and where support is needed.
  • Facilitation guides for leadership retreats, advisory councils, and board workshops focused on local accountability.
  • Sample board and community reports that integrate quantitative indicators with student artifacts and narratives.
  • References to toolkits (for example, “See Toolkit: Phase 3 Implementation Planning Guide” or “See Template: Local Evidence Conversation Protocol”).
Why this matters: Without practical supports, even a well designed system will stall. Implementation tools help districts move beyond vision to sustained, everyday use.

Research Base and References for Phase 3 Implementation

Phase 3 Implementation draws on research in implementation science, continuous improvement, data informed decision making, and professional learning. These fields emphasize that sustainable change depends on clear structures, iterative testing, and authentic engagement of the people who use new tools.

  • Bryk, A. S., Gomez, L. M., Grunow, A., & LeMahieu, P. G. (2015). Learning to Improve: How America's Schools Can Get Better at Getting Better. Supports the use of disciplined inquiry cycles and networked learning to strengthen implementation over time.
  • Fullan, M., & Quinn, J. (2016). Coherence: The Right Drivers in Action for Schools, Districts, and Systems. Highlights the importance of focused direction, collaborative cultures, deep learning, and accountability built on capacity building.
  • Hargreaves, A., & O’Connor, M. T. (2018). Collaborative Professionalism: When Teaching Together Means Learning for All. Emphasizes the role of collaborative structures and professional learning communities in sustaining new practices.
How to use this section: These references can support conversations with boards, advisory councils, and staff about why Phase 3 emphasizes implementation quality, collaboration, and continuous learning rather than one time rollout.
